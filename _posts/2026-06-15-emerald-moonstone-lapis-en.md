The maker has produced a third pendant in the same rough-gem, brass-channel style, and this time the stones have names from the beginning. No guessing games. The blue is **lapis lazuli** — the real thing, with that midnight blue and the faint pyrite glitter that I mistakenly assigned to a sapphire in the first batch. The green is **emerald**. The white stone in the middle is probably **moonstone**, judging by the soft blue glow that floats across its surface when the light hits it right.

Three stones, three colors, one rectangular brass tray. It looks like a flag for a small country that does not exist yet.

---

## The Emerald

The emerald is the loudest stone in the piece, which is saying something when lapis lazuli is also in the room. It is a deep, slightly uneven green, cut with rough facets that catch light in patches rather than in a clean flash. This is not the glassy, inclusion-free emerald you see in a display case under spotlights. This is an emerald that has kept its body: inclusions, uneven color, and a surface that says it was shaped by hand and judgment rather than by a precision cutting wheel.

In a conventional jewelry economy, this emerald would be considered flawed. Here, the flaws are the feature. The rough cut keeps the weight. The inclusions keep the identity. The uneven surface keeps it from looking like a piece of green glass.

![The pendant from another angle, moonstone showing blue adularescence](/assets/images/homemade-pendant-emerald-moonstone-lapis-2.jpg)

---

## The Moonstone

The middle stone is pale and slightly milky, and in certain angles it throws back a blue light from inside itself. That internal blue glow — called adularescence — is the signature of **moonstone**. It is the quietest stone in the pendant, the one that does not announce itself until the light moves.

Moonstone is a perfect choice for this design because it refuses to behave like a typical gem. It does not sparkle. It glows. It does not show off its facets. It shows off its depth. Between the assertive green of the emerald and the flat, ancient blue of the lapis, the moonstone acts as a kind of buffer — a pale, luminous pause.

![The pendant lying flat, the moonstone's blue flash visible](/assets/images/homemade-pendant-emerald-moonstone-lapis-3.jpg)

---

## The Lapis Lazuli

The two small blue rectangles at the end are **lapis lazuli**, the same stone the ancient Egyptians used for eye paint and the same stone Renaissance painters ground into ultramarine. It is a blue that does not need polishing to be convincing. It is simply, unmistakably blue.

What makes the lapis work here is its flatness. While the emerald and moonstone play with light, the lapis absorbs it. It is the anchor. It is the stone that says, stop looking for sparkle, the color is enough.

![The pendant tilted, showing the rough facets on the emerald and the depth of the lapis](/assets/images/homemade-pendant-emerald-moonstone-lapis-4.jpg)

---

## The Same Refusal

This pendant continues the same quiet rebellion as the others. It refuses to chase the 4Cs. It refuses to discard material for the sake of optical perfection. It refuses to pretend that brass is gold or that rough is unfinished.

The result is a piece that looks like it could have been made a thousand years ago or yesterday. The materials are old — emerald, lapis, moonstone — but the attitude is older: take what the earth gives you, cut it only as much as necessary, and set it in metal. The Han dynasty workshops would have understood the logic. The modern jewelry counter would not.
