A few weeks ago I wrote about Gobi eye agate as a specimen — a stone that carries the remnants of an ocean that dried up before dinosaurs existed. These four pieces are the same stone, but treated differently. Someone has drilled them, strung them on black cord with a small carnelian bead, and turned them into pendants. The eyes are no longer museum pieces. They are now meant to hang against a chest.

It is the right treatment. Gobi eye agate does not need a glass case. It needs to be carried around.

---

## The Brown Teardrop

The first pendant is a teardrop of warm brown and cream, covered in concentric eye rings that look like they were painted by a careful lunatic. The eyes vary in size — some are small and tight, others have opened into full pupils surrounded by white halos. The surface is not polished to a mirror. It is smooth enough to wear, rough enough to keep its desert character.

Held to the light, the stone glows amber-orange. The eyes become dark silhouettes floating in translucent chalcedony. The back side is simpler, more banded, as if the front used up all the drama and the back was left to rest.

![The brown teardrop pendant backlit, showing translucent amber and dark eye spots](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-pendant-brown-teardrop-2.jpg)

---

## The Orange Oval

The second piece is an oval the color of dried apricot and old ivory. The eye patterns here are larger and more spaced out, like the rings left by raindrops on a pond. There is a calmness to this one. The first pendant feels like it is watching you. This one feels like it has already seen everything and decided not to comment.

Flip it over and the pattern loosens into classic agate banding — ribbons of orange and white that ripple like sediment. Two faces, same stone, same silence.

## The Yellow Oval

The third pendant is the brightest of the four — a saturated yellow oval crowded with eyes of different sizes, like a sunflower head abstracted into stone. The eyes here are sharp and well-defined: dark centers, pale rings, then the yellow field. It is the most graphic piece, the one that looks least like a natural object and most like a design.

The reverse is quieter but still yellow, with fewer eyes and more flowing bands. It is the stone’s way of proving it is not just a print. The pattern goes all the way through.

## The Tan Teardrop

The fourth pendant returns to the teardrop shape, but in softer tan, cream, and brown. The eyes here are smaller and more numerous, packed so tightly that they begin to overlap like bubbles in foam. There is something map-like about it — contour lines, pools, landmasses. If you told me this stone was a fragment of an ancient navigational chart, I would believe you.

Its back continues the same vocabulary: bands, eyes, and tiny circular cells packed together. There is no blank side. The stone is busy from every angle.

## Why Wear Them

Collectors often keep Gobi eye agate in cabinets, sorted by size and pattern, treated like shrunken landscapes under glass. These four pieces reject that retirement. They have been drilled and corded, given a job. The black cord is the right frame — it does not compete. The small carnelian bead above each stone is a quiet nod to the agate’s neighbor in the chalcedony family.

Wearing one means carrying a few hundred million years against your skin. It also means accepting that the stone is more interesting than anything most people will wear next to it. That is the point. You do not wear Gobi eye agate to match an outfit. You wear it because the desert made something no human could repeat.
