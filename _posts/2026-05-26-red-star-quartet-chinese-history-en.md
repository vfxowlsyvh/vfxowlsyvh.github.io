![Red Star Tongji 1972 front view with red sunburst dial](/assets/images/red-star-collection-5.jpg)

Most microbrands borrow history from somewhere else. A dive watch that wishes it were a 1954 Blancpain. A chronograph chasing the ghost of a 1969 Zenith. A field watch cosplaying as a 1940s Dirty Dozen issue. It is a comfortable formula: pick a vintage reference, file off the serial numbers, add a Miyota or Seiko movement, and sell nostalgia by the kilo.

Red Star does something stranger. It mines its *own* history — specifically, the half-century of Chinese state-run watchmaking that produced the Tongji (Unified) movement, the 1963 Air Force chronograph, and an entire ecosystem of factories that most Western collectors have never heard of. The result is a quartet of watches that feel less like homages and more like historical fan fiction: "What if the Tianjin Watch Factory had made a regulator?" "What if the 1963 chronograph had spawned a dress line?"

Here are four of them, photographed individually, front and back, each with a different answer.

---

### The Cream Dual-Time Regulator (Zheng He)

![Cream dial regulator with power reserve and dual time sub-dials](/assets/images/red-star-collection-1.jpg)

This is the most intellectually ambitious of the group. A cream dial hosts two black sub-dials — each displaying its own time zone — with a power-reserve arc at six and a date track ringing the outer edge. A single red star sits at twelve, like a punctuation mark. It is a dual-time regulator: the left dial tracks a second time zone, the right dial keeps local time, and the long central hand sweeps the minutes for both. For a watch dedicated to a maritime explorer, the complication makes almost too much sense.

The caseback tells a stranger story than expected. Around the exhibition window, engraved in Chinese, runs the inscription *"国首位航海家 郑和"* — China's first navigator, Zheng He. The famed Ming Dynasty admiral who led seven maritime expeditions across the Indian Ocean is not exactly the first figure you associate with a mechanical wristwatch, yet here he is, immortalised on a caseback screwed down with chunky notches.

![Exhibition caseback showing Zheng He inscription and gold rotor](/assets/images/red-star-collection-2.jpg)

Through the glass, the automatic movement reveals a gold-toned rotor — but this is not the vintage Tongji workhorse one might assume. It is a new, precision automatic calibre with hand-winding capability, driving dual-time, date, and power-reserve complications. The finishing is noticeably cleaner than the industrial Tongji era, and the combination of features — dual-time plus regulator layout plus power reserve in a single Chinese movement — is unusual enough to suggest limited production rather than a mass-market calibre. You will not find this movement casually listed on AliExpress or Taobao.

The Zheng He regulator wears its history on its sleeve while running something far more modern underneath. The sapphire crystal and 5 bar water resistance feel like standard equipment for a properly engineered travel watch, not apologetic afterthoughts.

### The Blue Sunburst

![Blue sunburst dial with SEC and DATE sub-dials](/assets/images/red-star-collection-3.jpg)

If the cream regulator is a history thesis, the blue dial is a party invitation. The dial surface is not true guilloché — the cuts are stamped rather than engine-turned — but under direct light the radiating ridges do a convincing impression of one, throwing shadows that shift from navy to electric blue depending on the angle. Applied Arabic numerals in a warm, almost copper tone give it a 1920s art-deco energy. Two cream sub-dials handle small seconds at ten o'clock and the date at seven o'clock, while a fan-shaped power reserve arcs across the upper right quadrant near one o'clock. A red star anchors the dial just below two o'clock, as if the watch is patriotically saluting its own complication set.

This is Red Star operating without the safety net of historical justification. There is no 1970s reference to point to. It is simply a Chinese microbrand making a handsome, slightly eccentric dress watch with a power reserve complication and a dial texture that punches well above its weight class. The burgundy leather strap is an odd but effective pairing — it clashes just enough to be interesting.

![Solid caseback showing HAND WINDING engraving](/assets/images/red-star-collection-4.jpg)

The caseback is solid, not exhibition, and confirms what the thin profile suggested: this is a hand-winding movement. The engraved text advertises "HAND WINDING," "POWER RESERVE INDICATOR," "THREE DIMENSION DIAL DESIGN," and "SAPPHIRE DOUBLE CURVE ANTI-REFLECTION GLASS" — a maximalist approach to caseback marketing that Red Star clearly enjoys. There is no rotor, no automatic wizardry, just a manually wound calibre doing its job behind a wall of stainless steel.

### The Tongji 1972 (Red Dial)

![Red sunburst dial with Chinese text and red star](/assets/images/red-star-collection-5.jpg)

Here is the watch that actually carries the vintage movement. The deep red sunburst dial is stamped *"中国统机 1972"* — China's Unified Movement, 1972 — at three o'clock, leaving no doubt about what powers it. A red star sits at nine o'clock. Syringe hands, applied indices, and a red-tipped seconds hand keep the dial legible against the dark fumé background that fades from black at the edges to a rich wine-red at the centre.

This is not an Air Force chronograph homage. It is something rarer: a contemporary watch built around a genuine, vintage Tongji calibre. The solid caseback tells the full story.

![Solid caseback showing RED STAR 统一机 1969/1972 engraving](/assets/images/red-star-collection-6.jpg)

*RED STAR 统一机 1969/1972 NO. 1422 2022*. The Tongji movement was China's answer to the Swiss ebauche — a standardised calibre developed from 1969 and deployed across dozens of state factories from 1972 onward, ensuring that a watch made in Shanghai could be repaired with parts from Tianjin. It was communist industrial policy made mechanical. This particular example was assembled in 2022 — movement number 1422 — five decades after the original design was standardised. The caseback engraving carries a diagram of the movement, a mechanical portrait of the calibre inside.

The result is a time capsule wearing a modern dress-watch case. The vintage Tongji movement was never intended to be displayed through an exhibition window; it is a tool, not a spectacle. Red Star has dressed it up in a sunburst dial and a black textured strap, but the heartbeat is unmistakably 1972. It hacks, it hand-winds, and it ticks with the slightly irregular charm of a movement that was designed for reliability across a continent rather than COSC certification.

### The Bronze Chronograph (Salmon Dial)

![Bronze chronograph with salmon dial and pump pushers](/assets/images/red-star-collection-7.jpg)

The bronze-cased chronograph is the brawler of the group. Its salmon-orange dial, domed crystal, and pump pushers evoke 1940s German flieger chronographs — Hanhart, Tutima, the Luftwaffe-issue tools that pilots wore over their flight jackets. The case has already begun to oxidise, developing a dark, mottled patina that makes it look like it has been dug out of a barn rather than dispatched from a warehouse.

The dial carries Chinese characters — *中国制造* (Made in China) — under the twelve o'clock index, a quiet assertion of origin in a genre dominated by European nostalgia. Given the case architecture and sub-dial placement, it is almost certainly powered by a Sea-Gull ST1901 or one of its derivatives: a hand-wound, column-wheel chronograph movement that traces its lineage directly back to the same Venus 175 calibre that powered the original 1963 Chinese Air Force chronograph.

![Exhibition caseback showing chronograph movement](/assets/images/red-star-collection-8.jpg)

The exhibition caseback confirms the suspicion. Through the glass you can see the column wheel, the blued screws, the gear train, and the balance assembly doing its work. It is a busy, theatrical movement — all levers and wheels and polished steel — and it looks exactly like what it is: a Chinese-made chronograph calibre with genuine historical pedigree, visible for anyone who cares to look. This watch is wearing European cosplay on the outside while running Chinese genetics on the inside, and unlike the Tongji dress piece, it is not shy about showing them off.

The tan leather strap and thick crystal give it real wrist presence. It is heavy, warm-toned, and slightly aggressive. If the cream regulator is for boardrooms and the blue sunburst is for dinner parties, the bronze chronograph is for people who want their watch to look like it could start a fire.

### What They Mean Together

Collectively, these four watches represent something rare in affordable horology: a coherent national identity. Red Star is not pretending to be Swiss. It is not quietly swapping in a Chinese movement while hoping you will squint and see Rolex. It is loudly, sometimes clumsily, celebrating a specific thread of watchmaking history that runs through Tianjin, Shanghai, and the state-factory system of the 1960s and 70s.

The designs are uneven. The finishing is utilitarian. The marketing copy on the casebacks — "Glowly Date," "Three Dimension Dial Design" — reads like it was translated by someone who learned English from a technical manual and a thesaurus simultaneously. But the watches have something more valuable than polish: they have a point of view. They know where they come from. And they are not afraid to remind you, with every red star on every dial, exactly what that means.

*Four Red Star mechanical watches. Movements: new precision dual-time automatic, hand-winding with power reserve, vintage Tongji 1972, Sea-Gull ST1901 chronograph (inferred). Cases: stainless steel and bronze. All acquired from a German vendor. Notably, these appear to be export-market pieces — oddly unavailable through Chinese domestic channels or local e-commerce platforms, as if Red Star manufactures them specifically for foreign collectors and then forgets to tell its home market they exist.*
