![Three Chokin plates showing Japanese and maritime themes](/assets/images/chokin-plates-batch2-1.jpg)

In the United Kingdom, a pint of beer costs approximately four to six pounds. A cup of coffee costs three. These three Chokin plates, bought from a second-hand market, cost between two and five pounds each. They are cheaper than alcohol, cheaper than caffeine, and cheaper than the bus fare required to collect them. For that price, you receive a disk of brass or copper, approximately fifteen centimetres in diameter, covered in engraved metalwork so fine that individual strands of hair are visible on the figures, and individual planks are countable on the ships. The surface is not merely engraved. In places, it is enamelled — a vitreous layer fused to the metal at high temperature, still glossy after decades of storage, still resistant to the oxygen that dulls raw metal.

![Close-up showing the Cutty Sark clipper ship detail](/assets/images/chokin-plates-batch2-2.jpg)

One of the plates depicts the Cutty Sark, the British tea clipper that once raced around Cape Horn. It is an odd subject for a Japanese decorative art. Chokin, the technique of engraving metal and then applying gold, silver, or copper foil to create contrast, is traditionally associated with Japanese imagery: Mount Fuji, cranes, samurai, cherry blossoms. But the workshops that produced these plates in the 1970s and 1980s were export-oriented. They made what sold. If the British market wanted a clipper ship, the British market received a clipper ship, rendered with the same precision that a Japanese artisan would apply to a temple or a waterfall. The result is a cultural displacement: a British icon rendered through Japanese eyes, in Japanese metal, using Japanese patience.

![Detail of another plate showing architectural engraving](/assets/images/chokin-plates-batch2-3.jpg)

The enamelwork is what separates these plates from ordinary tourist trinkets. Enamel — specifically cloisonné or champlevé variants applied to Chokin pieces — requires multiple firings. The metal is engraved, the cavities are filled with powdered glass, and the piece is heated until the glass melts and bonds. Cool it too quickly and the enamel cracks. Heat it unevenly and the colours bleed. The process is unforgiving. Yet these plates, priced lower than a sandwich, survived it decades ago and remain flawless today. The red is still red. The blue is still blue. The gold foil has not tarnished because it was properly sealed beneath a transparent enamel layer. This is not craft that deserves neglect. It is craft that has been neglected by accident.

![Third plate showing landscape and architectural detail](/assets/images/chokin-plates-batch2-4.jpg)

The second-hand market does not know what to do with them. They arrive in charity shops and car-boot sales by the boxful, wrapped in newspaper, separated from the wooden stands that once held them upright. Sellers price them by weight or by diameter, as if they were copper blanks rather than finished objects. Buyers walk past because the style is unfashionable, because the subjects are unfamiliar, because the technique is invisible to anyone who has not looked closely enough to see that the lines are cut by hand and not stamped by machine. A machine can produce regularity. Only a hand can produce the slight waver in a ship's rigging that suggests wind, or the uneven depth in a mountain's slope that suggests distance.

There is an economic question here. Why does something that required multiple skilled processes — engraving, inlay, enamel firing, polishing — trade for less than the cost of the labour that produced it? Part of the answer is volume. These plates were produced in large numbers for the export market, and large numbers survive. Part of the answer is fashion. The aesthetic belongs to a period of interior decoration that has passed. Part of the answer is ignorance. Most people do not know what Chokin is, do not recognise the technique, and cannot distinguish a hand-engraved plate from a printed reproduction.

But the largest part of the answer is liquidity. A pint of beer is consumed immediately and must be replaced. A Chokin plate can be resold, gifted, or stored indefinitely. The low price is not a reflection of low quality. It is a reflection of low demand in a market that has not yet been taught to want these objects. That gap — between what something is and what the market believes it to be — is the space where value accumulates. The provenance thesis applies here too. A two-pound plate with a blog post is no longer a two-pound plate. It is a documented object with a known technique, a known origin, and a known reason for being interesting. The plate did not change. The information around it did.

*Three Chokin plates, acquired from UK second-hand markets. Brass or copper base, hand-engraved with gold and silver foil inlay. Partial enamel surfaces. Subjects include Japanese architecture and the British clipper Cutty Sark. Price range: two to five pounds each.*
