The sunburst teal dial catches light from a hundred metres. Not metaphorically — literally. The radial brushing throws colour like a signal beacon, and the custom Kaspa logo at ten o'clock confirms this is not a factory piece. It is a mod: an NH35 movement, an AliExpress dial, an independent case, and a pair of hands pressed into place by someone whose day job involves writing code.

Watch modding has become the hobby of choice for a particular demographic. Software developers, accustomed to building systems from discrete components, treat a mechanical watch the same way. They source movements, dials, cases, and bracelets from AliExpress and Cousins, then assemble in spare hours. The result is a cheap, customised object that carries more personal meaning than anything bought off a shelf. The community is large, vocal, and growing.

![Kaspa watch vertical angle](/assets/images/kaspa-watch-2.jpg)

What is less obvious is the economics. Two years ago, a Seiko NH35 could be had on AliExpress for roughly £25. The same movement now costs £50 or more. At UK stockists like Cousins, the price has climbed above £75. That is a threefold increase in under twenty-four months. The S&P 500 did not manage that. Neither did Rolex, whose secondary-market prices have softened across most references since the 2022 peak. A Seiko calibre, mass-produced and unloved by collectors, has outperformed both.

The explanation is not scarcity. Seiko still manufactures the NH35 in volume. The driver is cost-push inflation at the base of the supply chain: copper and steel prices, electricity tariffs, and labour costs in Southeast Asian assembly plants. A movement that contains no innovation, no new intellectual property, and no marketing budget has appreciated purely because the material and energy required to make it have become more expensive. The factory passes the cost downstream, and the mod community absorbs it. There is no alternative. If you want an automatic movement at this price point, the NH35 is the default.

![Kaspa watch flat lay](/assets/images/kaspa-watch-3.jpg)

This particular build sits in that context. The case is from an independent brand, polished to a mirror finish. The bracelet is a solid multi-link design with a pressed clasp. The bezel carries depth markings — a dive-watch convention applied here for visual weight rather than function. Total budget: comfortably under £500. For context, commissioning a single-piece custom watch from a manufacture like H. Moser & Cie. starts in the low hundreds of thousands. The gap is not a measure of quality difference; it is a measure of labour specialisation, brand premium, and the economics of hand-finishing. This watch achieves a comparable visual impact at roughly one one-thousandth of the cost.

![Kaspa watch caseback and bracelet](/assets/images/kaspa-watch-4.jpg)

The Kaspa branding is the personal touch. The teal dial matches the project's colour identity, and the logo replaces what would normally be a brand name or model designation. It is an object made for a community — not sold by one. In an era where every crypto project spawns merchandise of dubious quality, a mechanical watch assembled by hand carries a different weight. It required patience, dexterity, and the willingness to destroy a £50 movement if the hands were pressed on crooked.

That was the hardest part. Hand-setting is finicky work. The hour, minute, and seconds hands must be aligned perfectly, pressed onto their posts without bending or scratching, and cleared of the dial surface by fractions of a millimetre. Get it wrong and the hands snag, stopping the movement. Get it really wrong and the dial lacquer scars. There is no factory jig here, no robotic assembler. Just tweezers, a loupe, and steady hands.

![Kaspa watch crown view](/assets/images/kaspa-watch-5.jpg)

![Kaspa watch side profile](/assets/images/kaspa-watch-6.jpg)

The finished piece works. It keeps time within the NH35's rated tolerances, winds smoothly, and the date snaps over at midnight. Whether it outperforms the S&P 500 as a financial instrument is debatable. As a statement of intent — that raw materials, basic engineering, and individual labour can produce something that looks like it cost a hundred times more — it is already successful.
