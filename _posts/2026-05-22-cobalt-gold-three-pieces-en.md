Three objects, three materials, three countries, one colour story. Cobalt blue and gold is a pairing that refuses to go out of fashion — it has appeared on Chinese Ming porcelain, on Persian minarets, on Victorian tea sets, and on mid-century decorative glass. These three pieces carry that tradition forward in different ways.

---

**Carlton Ware Ashtray**

![Carlton Ware ashtray detail](/assets/images/cobalt-gold-trays-2.jpg)

The hexagonal body is stamped *Carlton Ware, Made in England* on the base. The ground is a saturated cobalt with a mottled, almost petrol-like quality that shifts under light. The decoration is pure chinoiserie: a pagoda on a rocky outcrop, water rendered in fine gold lines, a small boat, trees in green and orange enamel. The cigarette rests are gilded, as is the inner rim.

Carlton Ware produced this kind of lustre-finish Oriental ware from their Staffordshire kilns through much of the twentieth century. The pagoda motif was a staple — they understood that British buyers wanted the romance of the East without the price of actual Chinese export porcelain. The quality of the goldwork here is surprisingly good for mass production; the lines are fine and the enamel is smooth, not bubbled. It was made to be used, and the wear on the gold at the rests confirms that it was.

---

**Gold and Cobalt Glass Dish**

![Cobalt glass dish detail](/assets/images/cobalt-gold-trays-3.jpg)

The middle piece is heavier than it looks. The body is glass, not ceramic, with a deep cobalt core that reads almost purple in thin areas. The rim is undulating, scalloped in six places, and the entire upper surface is covered in gold: solid bands, a lattice panel, scrollwork, and hand-painted white flowers with green stems. The painting is freehand — the leaves are not identical, and the dot patterns vary slightly — which suggests workshop production rather than factory transfer.

The style is closest to mid-century Venetian or Bohemian glass, though without a base mark the exact origin is uncertain. What is clear is the intent: to make an object that reads as jewellery at table scale. The gold is applied over the glass, not within it, which means it will wear with handling. The piece may have started life as a bonbon dish or a vanity tray. Now it functions as a colour accent.

---

**Limoges Castel Handled Dish**

![Limoges Castel dish detail](/assets/images/cobalt-gold-trays-4.jpg)

The base is stamped *Limoges Castel, 22K Gold* inside a crest. This is hard-paste porcelain from the Limoges region of France, decorated with genuine 22-karat gold. The body is a true cobalt — darker and more uniform than the Carlton Ware — and the goldwork is restrained compared to the glass piece. A floral spray in the centre, a band of small flowers around the inner rim, and two elaborate rococo handles that are themselves cast metal, not porcelain.

![Bases of all three pieces](/assets/images/cobalt-gold-trays-5.jpg)

The contrast between the three bases is instructive. The Carlton Ware is fully glazed white stoneware. The glass piece has a ground pontil and no mark. The Limoges is unglazed porcelain around the foot, with the gold stamp sitting on the raw body — a standard Limoges presentation. Three different factories, three different pricing structures, none of which mattered at the point of purchase. Each of these was under twenty pounds. The Limoges stamp and the 22K gold claim might suggest high value, but the secondary market for decorative Limoges is soft — too much was produced, and too few people still set tables with handled dishes. The glass piece has no provenance at all. The Carlton Ware is collectible in theory but common in practice. The lesson is that cobalt and gold reads as luxury regardless of price. The materials do the work. You do not need to spend much to own the colour.

---

What holds them together is not origin or function but chromatic confidence. Cobalt blue does not apologise. It is the colour of lapis lazuli, of Yves Klein, of the deepest ocean. Paired with gold, it becomes declarative. These three objects were made decades apart, in different factories, for different markets, yet they agree on that one thing: blue this dark needs gold to lift it. And the lift costs less than you think.
