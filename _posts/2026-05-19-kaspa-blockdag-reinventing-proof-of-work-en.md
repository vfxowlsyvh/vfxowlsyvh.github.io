While Bitcoin processes one block every ten minutes, Kaspa produces **ten blocks every second** — and plans to reach one hundred. This is not marketing hyperbole. It is the result of replacing the traditional blockchain with a BlockDAG, a structure that allows multiple miners to create valid blocks simultaneously without wasting computational effort.

Kaspa was founded by Yonatan Sompolinsky, a researcher whose early work on the PHANTOM protocol directly influenced Ethereum's design. The network launched on November 7, 2021, with **zero premine, zero ICO, and zero venture capital allocation**. Every KAS token in existence was earned through proof-of-work mining, making it one of the most fairly distributed cryptocurrencies since Bitcoin itself.

The technical breakthrough is GHOSTDAG, a consensus protocol that orders blocks in a directed acyclic graph rather than discarding competing blocks. In Bitcoin, when two miners solve a block at nearly the same time, one chain wins and the other is orphaned. That wasted electricity is simply the cost of doing business. Kaspa eliminates this waste by accepting all valid blocks into the ledger, ordering them retrospectively through a greedy heaviest-subtree algorithm.

This matters for more than just efficiency. With ten blocks per second, Kaspa achieves **sub-second transaction inclusion** and approximately ten seconds for full probabilistic security. That is roughly six thousand times faster than Bitcoin's block rate. The project roadmap targets thirty-two blocks per second next, with a long-term goal of one hundred.

Kaspa uses the **kHeavyHash** algorithm, which involves heavy matrix multiplication and is notably compatible with optical mining — a future technology that could dramatically reduce energy consumption. The algorithm was initially GPU-friendly, but ASIC miners from Bitmain and IceRiver now dominate the network. Difficulty recently hit approximately sixty-one petahash, near an all-time high, signaling strong infrastructure commitment despite volatile market conditions.

Unlike Bitcoin's four-year halving cycle, Kaspa employs a **chromatic emission schedule** inspired by the musical scale. Block rewards halve smoothly every month, reducing by a factor of roughly one-half to the power of one-twelfth. The max supply is capped at 28.7 billion KAS, with 100% of emissions flowing to miners — no developer tax, no foundation allocation, no staking rewards.

What makes Kaspa particularly interesting in 2026 is its positioning. While Bitcoin miners flee to AI data centers to survive profitability pressure, Kaspa's ecosystem is still growing. The network hashrate is rising, new ASIC hardware is shipping, and the community is expanding. There is no institutional baggage, no Wall Street ETF, no regulatory circus. It is pure proof-of-work, rebuilt from first principles.

BlockDAGs are not without skepticism. Critics argue that higher block rates create storage and bandwidth challenges, and that the security model, while theoretically sound, has not faced the same decade of adversarial testing as Bitcoin's Nakamoto consensus. These are fair concerns. But Kaspa represents something rare in crypto: a genuine architectural innovation that does not sacrifice decentralization for speed.

Proof-of-work does not have to be slow. Kaspa is proving that.
