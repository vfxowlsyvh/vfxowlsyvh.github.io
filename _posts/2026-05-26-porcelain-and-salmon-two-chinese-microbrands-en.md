The Chinese microbrand ecosystem is not a single thing. It is a continent. At one end you have Kickstarter-funded studios chasing porcelain dials and Jingdezhen craftsmanship. At the other you have Beijing workshops stamping out salmon-coloured dress watches for less than the price of a decent fountain pen. These two watches share a country of origin and little else.

---

### Halcyon Journey: Porcelain from Jingdezhen

![Halcyon Journey white porcelain dial with Roman numerals and blued hands](/assets/images/microbrand-halcyon-front.jpg)

Halcyon is a Singapore-based brand, but its soul is in Jingdezhen — the porcelain capital of China, a city that has been firing ceramic for over a thousand years. The Journey is their flagship: a 40mm dress watch built around a genuine porcelain dial, powered by a Miyota 9039 automatic movement, and priced at around $800 USD.

The dial is the story. Porcelain is not enamel. Enamel is a coating; porcelain is a material. It begins as clay containing kaolinite, is shaped, and then fired at temperatures high enough to vitrify it into something that resists water, sunlight, and most chemicals. For a watch dial, the tolerance is brutal: the surface must be flatter than 0.02 mm, and once fired, porcelain cannot be corrected. The failure rate in production can reach 90%. What survives is a milky-white surface with a soft, almost organic glow — not reflective like enamel, not flat like painted metal. It simply breathes.

The Roman numerals on this example are crisp and printed with high-end precision — no bleeding, even under magnification. The hands are heat-blued, not painted, which gives them a depth that changes from navy to electric blue depending on the light. The case is 316L stainless steel, 10.3 mm thick, with a double-stepped bezel and a push-pull crown that keeps the silhouette elegant. The solid caseback is utilitarian; this is not a watch that wants to show off its Miyota rotor.

Halcyon ran a Kickstarter campaign in mid-2024 with a modest S$30,000 goal. They raised S$121,955 from 124 backers — enough to prove that a market exists for Chinese porcelain craftsmanship translated into wristwatch form. The brand sells direct-to-consumer, covering duties and offering 30-day returns, which is the standard microbrand playbook. What is not standard is the material. You do not see true porcelain dials at this price from European brands. You barely see them at double the price.

### Fan Zhi (凡致): Salmon for the Masses

![Fan Zhi salmon dial with applied Arabic numerals and red strap](/assets/images/microbrand-fanzhi-front.jpg)

If Halcyon is the porcelain boutique, Fan Zhi — also sold internationally as Merkeur or Merkur — is the discount warehouse with surprising taste. This is a Beijing-based brand that operates primarily through Taobao and AliExpress, offering mechanical watches at prices that start around ¥400 RMB and rarely exceed ¥900. That is roughly £50 to £110. For that money you get a hand-wound mechanical movement, a sapphire crystal, and — in this case — a salmon dial that would not look out of place on a watch costing twenty times as much.

The dial is a warm copper-salmon with applied Arabic numerals at the even hours and baton indices at the odd. The text reads *"凡致"* at twelve o'clock and *"中國製"* (Made in China) at six — a quiet assertion of origin that many Chinese brands still shy away from. The dauphine hands are polished and catch light well. The case is a simple 36–38mm cylinder with brushed and polished surfaces, wearing smaller than the Halcyon but feeling more vintage-appropriate because of it.

The caseback is more interesting than the movement it hides. A solid steel disc is engraved with a mountain logo and Chinese characters, surrounded by a ring of text: *"EVERY SHOUT IN XIANG JI ZHEN BAO SHOU DOU JIAN FENG ZHEN"*. It is not clear if this is a proverb, a brand motto, or simply decorative English filtered through a translation algorithm. Either way, it has more personality than the generic wave logos you see on most cheap dive watches.

The movement inside is a hand-wound Chinese calibre — likely a Sea-Gull ST3600 or similar derivative of the Unitas 6497 architecture. It is not decorated. It is not accurate to COSC standards. It does not need to be. It hand-winds, it ticks, and it costs less than a meal for two in London. The red leather strap on this example is an aftermarket choice that clashes productively with the salmon dial, giving the watch a vaguely Italian-racing energy that the brand probably did not intend.

### The Gap Between Them

These two watches are separated by roughly $700 and an entire philosophy. The Halcyon is a material experiment — a bet that Chinese porcelain craftsmanship can justify a mid-tier price point if packaged correctly. The Fan Zhi is a colour experiment — a bet that a salmon dial and a hand-wound movement can make a £70 watch feel special.

Both bets work, in their own way. The Halcyon justifies its price every time the porcelain dial catches window light and glows instead of reflecting. The Fan Zhi justifies its price every time someone asks where you got it and you can honestly say, "A Chinese website, for seventy pounds."

What connects them is honesty. Halcyon does not pretend its Miyota 9039 is exotic. Fan Zhi does not pretend its case finishing is Swiss. They simply offer two different things that Western microbrands rarely attempt at these price points: genuine porcelain from a millennium-old craft centre, and unapologetic salmon at impulse-buy pricing.

*Halcyon Journey: 40mm, Jingdezhen porcelain dial, Miyota 9039 automatic, heat-blued hands, ~$819 USD. Fan Zhi (Merkeur): ~36mm, salmon dial, hand-wound Chinese movement, ~¥570-899 RMB.*
