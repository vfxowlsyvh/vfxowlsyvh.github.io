On November 15, 2018, Bitcoin Cash split in two. The triggering dispute was block size — specifically, whether to raise the limit from 32 MB to 128 MB. But the real conflict was theological. One faction, led by Roger Ver and Jihan Wu, believed Bitcoin Cash should evolve cautiously, preserving its payment-network function. The other faction, led by Craig Wright and Calvin Ayre, believed the original Bitcoin protocol was essentially perfect and should simply be scaled upward without alteration. The latter camp called itself **Bitcoin Satoshi Vision (BSV)**. The hash war lasted two weeks, cost an estimated $12 million in electricity, and ended with BSV as a separate chain.

What followed was not a contest for market share but a campaign for legitimacy. Wright had been claiming since 2016 that he was Satoshi Nakamoto. The BSV project was built on this foundation: if Wright invented Bitcoin, then BSV — by enforcing the "original protocol" at massive scale — was the one true chain. Every other fork, including BTC itself, was a deviation. This was not a technical argument. It was a genealogical one.

The courts became the battlefield. In 2021, the Kleiman v. Wright trial in Florida examined whether Wright had stolen Bitcoin from his deceased business partner. The jury found Wright liable for $100 million in intellectual property theft but did not rule on the Satoshi question. In 2024, the Crypto Open Patent Alliance (COPA) brought a UK lawsuit specifically to determine whether Wright was Satoshi. The judge ruled comprehensively against Wright, finding that he had forged documents, lied repeatedly under oath, and was not Satoshi Nakamoto. Wright disappeared from public view shortly after. Ayre remained, funding the ecosystem through his gambling fortune and a network of affiliated companies including nChain and TAAL.

Technically, BSV did what it promised. The block size limit was removed entirely, then reinstated at progressively higher ceilings — 2 GB, then 4 GB. The chain now processes more data per block than Bitcoin processes in a month. But throughput without demand is just empty storage. BSV's transaction count is dominated by a handful of applications — weather data uploads, social media posts, token protocols — running on infrastructure largely controlled by Ayre-affiliated entities. Decentralization was sacrificed for scale. The hashrate hovers around 1 EH/s, roughly 0.1% of Bitcoin's, secured by a small number of mining pools.

The market's verdict was brutal. Between 2019 and 2021, major exchanges delisted BSV en masse — Binance, Kraken, ShapeShift, Bittrex, and others. The reasons varied: some cited Wright's legal harassment of critics, others cited low trading volume, others simply wanted no part of the drama. By 2024, BSV was unavailable on most regulated platforms. It trades on a handful of smaller exchanges at prices that fluctuate wildly on thin volume. The market cap, once briefly in the top five, has fallen out of the top fifty.

Yet BSV persists. Not because it is winning, but because it is funded. Ayre's money keeps the servers running, the conferences happening, the legal teams employed. The chain produces blocks every ten minutes, exactly as designed. The difficulty adjusts. The coins move. It is, by any technical definition, a functioning proof-of-work blockchain. It is also a monument to something else: the belief that conviction, backed by sufficient capital, can sustain a network against near-universal rejection from the industry that spawned it.

Holding BSV is not a bet on adoption. It is a bet on memory. The 2018 hash war was the most expensive and visible conflict in cryptocurrency's short history. The COPA trial produced a judicial record that will be cited for decades. The delistings established precedents for how exchanges handle controversial forks. BSV is the artifact of all of this — a chain that exists because someone refused to let it die, and because the mathematics of proof-of-work do not care about popularity. A hash is a hash. A valid block is a valid block. The network does not know it is unwanted.

*BSV. Forked from Bitcoin Cash November 15, 2018. SHA-256 proof-of-work. 10-minute blocks. No hard block size cap. Delisted from most major exchanges 2019–2021. COPA trial concluded March 2024: Craig Wright found not to be Satoshi Nakamoto.*
