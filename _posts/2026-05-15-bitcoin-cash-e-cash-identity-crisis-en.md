On August 1, 2017, the Bitcoin blockchain split in two. The Big Block camp, led by figures like Roger Ver and Jihan Wu, forked away to create **Bitcoin Cash (BCH)**—a network that raised the block size limit from 1 MB to 32 MB. Their argument was simple: if Bitcoin was meant to be peer-to-peer electronic cash, it needed to process more than seven transactions per second and charge less than a dollar in fees. Eight years later, that vision is being tested by forces no one fully anticipated.

Bitcoin Cash has stayed true to its original mission in the most literal sense. Transactions still cost less than a cent, and the network handles roughly 100 TPS with instant zero-confirmation settlements. For merchants in Nigeria, the Philippines, and Mozambique, BCH remains a practical rail for remittances and micro-payments. In 2025 alone, cross-border volume through BCH reached approximately $350 million, with fees roughly 80% lower than traditional banking corridors.

Yet the ground has shifted beneath it. Stablecoins—USDC, USDT, and their derivatives—have captured the payment use case BCH once owned. In 2025, stablecoin remittance volume was twenty-five times larger than BCH's. A merchant in Lagos can now accept USDC on Ethereum Layer 2 with near-instant finality and no price volatility. That convenience is hard to beat.

On the mining side, BCH offers an interesting wrinkle. Because it shares Bitcoin's SHA-256 algorithm, ASIC miners can switch between the two chains based on profitability. As of early 2026, BCH has actually offered a roughly 5% profitability premium over BTC for SHA-256 miners, helping stabilize hashrate around 8.5 EH/s. That is only about 1% of Bitcoin's total security budget, but it has proven sufficient to keep the chain running without incident since launch.

The most significant development on the horizon is the **Layla upgrade**, scheduled for May 15, 2026—today, in fact. Layla introduces bounded loops, bitwise operations, and function definitions to Bitcoin Cash's scripting engine. Combined with the CashTokens feature launched in 2023, BCH is attempting to evolve from "digital cash" into something closer to "programmable Bitcoin." Whether developers actually build on this UTXO-based smart contract platform remains the open question.

Institutional interest, meanwhile, remains tepid. The Grayscale Bitcoin Cash Trust trades at a 16.4% discount to net asset value, and no major public corporation holds BCH on its balance sheet. The SEC did classify Bitcoin Cash as a digital commodity in March 2026, providing regulatory clarity, but clarity without capital is just a label.

BCH now occupies an uncomfortable middle ground. It is too volatile to compete with stablecoins for everyday payments, too under-capitalized to challenge Bitcoin as a store of value, and too early in its smart-contract journey to rival Ethereum or Solana. Yet the chain has survived two contentious splits—Bitcoin SV in 2018 and eCash in 2020—and still maintains the fourth-largest merchant acceptance footprint among all cryptocurrencies.

The Layla upgrade is a bet that utility, not narrative, will determine survival. If developers build compelling DeFi applications on BCH's fast, cheap settlement layer, the electronic cash dream may yet find a second act. If not, Bitcoin Cash risks becoming a historical artifact: the chain that was right about scaling, but wrong about what users actually wanted.
