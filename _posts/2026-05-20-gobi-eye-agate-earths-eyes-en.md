![Gobi eye agate close-up showing concentric eye patterns](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-1.jpg)

Hold this stone in your palm and you are holding the remnants of an ocean that dried up before dinosaurs walked the earth. These concentric rings—brown pupils surrounded by white sclera, set in olive-green skin—are not carvings. They are **Gobi eye agate**, formed between 150 and 300 million years ago when submarine volcanoes erupted beneath a sea that no longer exists.

![Gobi eye agate with 20p coin for scale](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-2.jpg)

The geology reads like a thriller. Molten basalt poured into cold seawater, trapping gas bubbles that became hollow vesicles. Over millennia, silica-rich groundwater seeped into these cavities, depositing microscopic layers of chalcedony and quartz. Trace elements—iron for the rust-orange rings, manganese for the dark centers—painted each band a different hue. Then the sea vanished. The desert came. And for millions of years, wind and sand polished what the water had made.

The result is this: a pebble barely larger than a twenty-pence coin, covered in eyes that seem to stare back. In Chinese collecting circles, Gobi agate is called the *śarīra* of the earth—a sacred relic forged from the essence of heaven, earth, sun, and moon. The eye formations are the most prized variety. Each "eye" is a frozen moment of mineral crystallization, a snapshot of chemistry written in concentric code.

![Banded side of Gobi eye agate](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-3.jpg)

Flip the stone over and the story changes. The reverse face abandons the ocular motif for classic banded agate patterns—alternating ribbons of green, white, and umber that ripple like sedimentary strata. This is the same stone wearing two faces: one surreal and figurative, the other geological and abstract. Both were shaped entirely without human intervention.

![Gobi eye agate top-down view](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-4.jpg)

The market for these stones has gone mad. Around 2004, herders in Inner Mongolia's Alxa League began collecting surface agates as a side income, selling bags for 30 to 50 yuan each. By 2005, over 1,200 tons had flooded markets across China. Then the collectors arrived—from Japan, the United States, Singapore, Hong Kong, Taiwan. Prices exploded. A 90-gram stone resembling a chick hatching from its shell was appraised at 130 million yuan. Another, depicting an old woman's face at 10×15×8 cm, reached 96 million yuan. A dealer who passed on an 8,000-yuan stone returned a year later to find it had sold for 28,000.

![Gobi eye agate full face detail](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-5.jpg)

Today, the easy pickings are gone. Predatory mining stripped surface deposits within a decade. Diggers now excavate three feet down, but sub-surface stones lack the lustrous wind-polish of their surface cousins. Quality specimens have vanished into private collections. The Inner Mongolia government has imposed restrictions on large-scale export, recognizing what was lost too late. The stone in your hand may be one of the last of its kind to leave the desert casually.

Collectors speak of five essential qualities in Gobi agate: *form, texture, color, grain, and charm*. This piece carries all five. The eyes give it form and charm. The wind-polished patina gives it texture. The spectrum from moss green to burnt umber gives it color. And the microscopic crystalline structure of each ring—quartz and moganite interlaced like carbon's diamond-graphite duality—gives it grain.

There is something unnerving about holding 300 million years in a palm-sized package. These eyes watched oceans drain and mountains rise. They weathered ice ages and the birth of mammals. Now they rest on a desk in the twenty-first century, stared at by a species that has existed for less than one-tenth of one percent of the stone's lifetime. The Gobi eye agate does not care about our appreciation. It simply is. That indifference is precisely what makes it magnificent.
