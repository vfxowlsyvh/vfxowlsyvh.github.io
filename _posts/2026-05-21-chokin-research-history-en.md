Chokin is not a single technique. It is a family of techniques, a cluster of methods for persuading metal to hold an image. The word itself — 彫金 — simply means "carved metal." But within that simplicity is a history that begins in the Yayoi period, around three hundred BCE, when metalworking arrived in Japan from continental Asia, and continues through the present day in workshops where chisels still bear the names of families who have used them for twenty generations.

![Close-up of engraved and inlaid surface detail](/assets/images/chokin-research-2.jpg)

The core tool is the tagane: a chisel with an edge measured in fractions of a millimetre. The artisan holds it against copper, brass, or shakudo — a copper-gold alloy that darkens to a near-black purple when treated — and strikes the other end with a small hammer. Each blow removes almost nothing. A single line in a hair-engraving (kebori) might require fifty strikes. A single plate might contain kilometres of lines. The work is not subtractive in the way that sculpture is subtractive. It is accumulative. The artist does not reveal a form hidden in the metal. The artist builds the form, strike by strike, by removing what is not the form.

The inlay technique (zogan) is where the real complexity lives. In Kaga zogan, the style most associated with high Chokin work, the artisan cuts a cavity into the base metal at an angle, so the bottom is wider than the opening. A sheet of gold or silver, softer than the base, is hammered into this undercut. The metal spreads at the bottom and locks into place. The overhanging edges of the base metal are then pressed down over the inlay, securing it without solder, without adhesive, without any bonding agent except geometry and force. The result is flush — the inlay and the base form a single continuous surface — and permanent. A well-executed Kaga inlay will outlast the object that contains it.

![Detail showing gold foil inlay and patinated copper](/assets/images/chokin-research-3.jpg)

Colour in Chokin is not applied. It is induced. The technique called irogane involves treating copper alloys with weak acidic solutions — copper sulfate, vinegar, sake lees — to create oxide layers of controlled thickness. A thin layer reflects red. A thicker layer reflects brown. A very thick layer goes to black. These are not paints. They are the metal's own skin, altered chemically. When sealed with wax or lacquer, they remain stable for centuries. When left exposed, they continue to shift, darkening gradually in response to the oils on human fingers and the sulfur in the air.

Historically, Chokin served power. From the twelfth century onward, the decoration of samurai armor, swords, and horse tack was a major patronage category. A tsuba — the guard between blade and grip — was not merely functional. It was a surface for displaying the owner's wealth, taste, and political allegiance. The Goto family, beginning with Yujo in the fifteenth century, defined the aesthetic language of sword furniture for two centuries. Their work is now in museums and private collections, valued not because the swords are useful but because the metalwork transcended its purpose. In 1876, the Meiji government banned the public wearing of swords. The craft nearly died. Metalworkers who had spent their lives engraving guards and pommels found themselves without a market. Many turned to art objects for export. The West, newly fascinated by Japan after the forced opening of the country, bought everything: vases, boxes, ornaments, and eventually the decorative plates that now sell for two pounds in British charity shops.

![Maritime architectural subject on Chokin plate](/assets/images/chokin-research-4.jpg)

The plates I collect are products of this export tradition. They were made in the 1970s and 1980s, in workshops that combined hand-engraving with semi-mechanised production. The designs were standardised. The base metals were pre-cut. But the engraving and inlay were still done by hand, because no machine at the time could replicate the subtle irregularities that make Chokin legible as craft. A machine produces identical lines. A hand produces lines that vary slightly in depth, in angle, in spacing — variations that the eye reads as life. These plates are not unique artworks. They are industrial craft, made in quantity for foreign buyers who wanted something Japanese without paying for something unique. That industrial origin is why they are cheap. It is also why they are honest. They do not pretend to be one-of-a-kind masterpieces. They pretend to be exactly what they are: metal, gold, silver, and human patience, compressed into a flat circle.

In 1955, the Japanese government designated Chokin techniques as an Important Intangible Cultural Heritage. In 2008, Katsura Morihito was recognised as a Living National Treasure for his mastery of metal chasing. These honours apply to the tradition, not to the export plates. But the tradition and the plates share a material continuity. The same chisels, the same alloys, the same physical principles of inlay and patination. The difference is context: one was made for a museum, the other for a mail-order catalogue. The metal does not know the difference. Only the market does.

The culture of Chokin is a culture of restraint. The samurai who commissioned sword furniture understood that display should be subtle — wealth signalled through detail rather than scale. The export plates invert this principle: they are large, flat, designed to be seen from across a room. But the technique remains restrained. The gold is thin. The colours are muted. The engraving does not shout. Even in its commercialised form, Chokin retains the aesthetic DNA of its origin: the belief that metal, properly worked, can carry meaning without demanding attention.

*Chokin plates. Brass and copper bases with hand-engraved gold and silver foil inlay. Techniques include kebori (hairline engraving) and zogan (metal inlay). Produced in Japan during the 1970s–1980s export era. Subjects: Japanese architecture, maritime scenes, and landscape.*
