![Bone china pheasant figurine on white surface](/assets/images/bone-china-pheasant-1.jpg)

The common pheasant is not common where it began. *Phasianus colchicus* — the name means "bird of the river Phasis," the Rioni in western Georgia, where the Caucasus meets the Black Sea — is native to a band of Asia that stretches from the Caucasus through the Himalayas to China, Korea, and Japan. The Romans carried it to Britain around AD 100. The Normans re-established it in the eleventh century. Now, two thousand years later, there are approximately eight million living in the British countryside, and something like twenty million more are bred and released annually to be shot. It is the most numerous game bird in the country. It is also, by some counts, the most frequently road-killed.

![Angled view showing the pheasant's pose and tail](/assets/images/bone-china-pheasant-2.jpg)

I hit one on the A1, a few years ago. Time did not behave normally. The last moments before impact expanded — not in the memory, but in the experience itself. The bird turned its head. The eye was visible. Then the eye was the only thing visible. Then the windscreen. This is a known neurological event: under extreme stress, the amygdala hijacks the prefrontal cortex and the brain records detail at a rate that exceeds normal playback speed. The result is the subjective experience of time slowing down. A second becomes an hour. A glance becomes a study. What you see in that elongated moment is stored differently from ordinary memory. It does not fade on the same schedule.

![Close-up of the pheasant's head and beak](/assets/images/bone-china-pheasant-3.jpg)

I bought this figurine because the pose matched. The head is turned slightly, the body low to the ground, the tail fanned in a way that suggests alarm or landing or the moment before flight. The artist who modelled it — working in bone china in an English factory, probably in the mid-twentieth century — had seen the same posture. Whether from life or from another dead bird does not matter. The angle of the neck, the set of the wings, the curve of the tail: these are not generic pheasant shapes. They are specific to a pheasant that has just realised it is in the wrong place. The artist preserved that specific moment in clay and bone ash, fired it at twelve hundred degrees, and glazed it. The result is permanent. The moment it represents is not.

![Rear view showing tail plumage detail](/assets/images/bone-china-pheasant-4.jpg)

Bone china is an English invention, developed in the eighteenth century by adding cattle bone ash to the standard porcelain recipe of kaolin and feldspar. It is whiter, more translucent, and more durable than ordinary porcelain. The bone content gives it a particular resonance — when tapped, it rings rather than clinks. A pheasant made of bone china is therefore a pheasant made, in part, of processed bone. The species that the Romans introduced to be hunted, that the Victorians bred in captivity, that the modern shooting industry releases by the million, is now memorialised in a material that contains the calcined remains of domestic animals. The symbolism is not intentional. But it is accurate.

The figurine does not judge. It is not a protest object. It is a recording device — a way of fixing a moment that the brain, for all its neurochemical urgency, cannot fully archive. The eye I saw on the A1 is not recoverable. The figurine's eye, painted in enamel and fixed under glaze, is always available. It looks in the same direction. It carries the same angle of attention. The difference is that one eye was alive and afraid, and the other is ceramic and indifferent. The figurine does not remember the impact. I do. It simply sits on a shelf, receiving light, while I carry the elongated second.

*Phasianus colchicus*. Native to Asia. Introduced to Britain by the Romans. Resident population: approximately eight million. Annual release for shooting: approximately twenty million. Road mortality: unquantified but significant. Bone china figurine, mid-twentieth century English manufacture. Bone ash, kaolin, feldspar, enamel. Survives. The bird does not.
