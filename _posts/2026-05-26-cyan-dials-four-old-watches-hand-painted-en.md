There is a particular madness to painting your own watch dials. It requires dissolving the factory finish, carefully preparing the surface, applying pigment by hand, and then reassembling a movement that was never designed to be disassembled by an amateur. Most people who attempt it ruin the first dial. Many ruin the second. The ones who keep going do so because they have a colour in mind that no factory will ever produce.

Here are four old watches — two mechanical, two quartz — that have been given new faces in cyan. Some use gold foil suspended in paint. Others use mineral pigments alone. All of them were painted by the same pair of hands, driven by the same fixation.

---

### The Gold Polygon (Quartz, Gold Foil)

![Gold polygonal case with black strap and gold-flecked cyan dial](/assets/images/cyan-dial-1.jpg)

The first victim is a 1970s-style quartz watch in a chunky gold polygonal case. The original dial was almost certainly brown or champagne — the safe choices of that era. Now it is a mottled cyan pool with gold foil flakes suspended throughout, like someone dropped a constellation into tropical water. The gold hands and gold case frame the dial in a way that should clash but does not; the warmth of the metal and the coldness of the cyan reach an uneasy truce.

![Angled view showing the polygonal bezel](/assets/images/cyan-dial-2.jpg)

The dial surface is not flat. The gold foil creates tiny ridges and valleys that catch light differently at every angle. Under direct sun, the dial flashes green-gold. In shade, it sinks into deep teal. There are no indices, no numerals, no branding — just the foil, the paint, and the hands. It is the most abstract of the four, closer to a jewellery brooch than a functional timepiece. The quartz movement inside keeps quiet time, unconcerned that its face has been turned into a painting.

---

### The Explorer Homage (Mechanical, Mineral Cyan)

![Steel Explorer-style case with cyan dial and orange GMT hand](/assets/images/cyan-dial-3.jpg)

The second watch wears its influences openly. The case is a vintage Explorer II homage — steel, 24-hour bezel, Mercedes hands, magnified date window at three o'clock. The original dial was probably black or white. Now it is an aggressive, saturated cyan that makes the orange GMT hand look like a rescue beacon against a tropical sea.

![Angled view showing the 24-hour bezel](/assets/images/cyan-dial-4.jpg)

This one uses mineral cyan paint without gold foil, and the result is cleaner, more uniform, almost industrial. The dial surface is smooth enough that the applied indices and lume plots sit properly — a practical concern that the gold-foil dial does not have to worry about. The old mechanical movement inside (date function, hacking seconds) was clearly designed for legibility in harsh conditions. Now it is legible against a colour that nature never intended for aviation or exploration.

The contrast between the tool-watch case architecture and the art-supply dial colour is the whole point. This is a watch that wants to be taken seriously and then undermines itself with every glance.

---

### The Seiko Diver (Automatic, Gold Indices)

![Gold diver case with orange strap and gold-painted indices](/assets/images/cyan-dial-6.jpg)

The third is the most technically complex. A gold-toned diver case with a rotating bezel, day-date window, and an orange leather strap that has no business looking this good against a cyan dial. The indices are not factory-applied; they are hand-painted in gold, matching the hands, giving the dial a deliberate, almost Art Deco symmetry.

![Exhibition caseback showing Seiko automatic movement](/assets/images/cyan-dial-5.jpg)

Through the exhibition caseback, a Seiko automatic movement does its work — gold-toned rotor, signed bridges, the reliable architecture that powered a thousand affordable watches in the 1980s and 90s. It is not a decorated movement. There are no Geneva stripes, no heat-blued screws, no exhibition-worthy finishing. But it is honest. It self-winds, it drives the day and date wheels, and it does not know that the dial above it has been repainted by hand in a colour Seiko never offered.

The day-date window is particularly charming. "TUE 29" peeks out from a field of cyan and gold, as if the calendar function is trying to maintain some dignity while surrounded by chaos.

---

### The White Minimalist (Quartz, Plain Cyan)

![White minimal case with green strap and plain cyan dial](/assets/images/cyan-dial-7.jpg)

The fourth and final watch is the quietest. A plain white round case, a green suede strap, and a dial that is nothing but cyan — no indices, no numerals, no date, no seconds hand. Just hour and minute hands floating on a disc of mineral paint.

![Alternative angle showing the green strap](/assets/images/cyan-dial-8.jpg)

This is the one that most clearly reveals the texture of the paint. The cyan was applied in layers, and the brush marks are faintly visible if you look closely, like wood grain under varnish. The green strap is an almost perfect colour match for the dial's darker undertones, creating a monochromatic effect that makes the white case pop like a picture frame.

It is the least practical watch of the four. Telling the exact time requires guessing, since there are no markers. But practicality was never the goal. The goal was to see what would happen if you stripped a watch down to its absolute minimum and then painted the only remaining surface in the most distracting colour possible.

---

### What They Mean Together

These four watches have nothing in common except the person who painted them and the colour they chose. The movements are old. The cases are cheap. The straps are aftermarket. None of them would impress a collector at a watch meet. But that is not the point.

The point is that watch dials are just flat surfaces waiting for someone to care about them. Factories paint them by the thousand, optimising for mass appeal and brand consistency. An individual with a brush and a bottle of cyan pigment can undo all of that in an afternoon. These four watches are not better than they were before. They are simply more specific. They now belong to one person, in one colour, for one reason: because cyan was the right answer.

*Four hand-painted watch dials. Two old mechanical movements (one Seiko automatic), two quartz movements. Mineral cyan paint and gold foil. All cases vintage or second-hand. Straps aftermarket.*
