![Cloisonné dragon box open side view](/assets/images/cloisonne-dragon-box-1.jpg)

The body is barely taller than a twenty-pence piece, yet the dragon coils around it with full theatrical commitment. Cloisonné at this scale is a test of patience: the wires must be bent and soldered to form cells barely a few millimetres across, then filled with enamel paste that shrinks in the kiln and often needs a second or third firing to sit level. This little cylindrical box passes the test reasonably well. The wire walls are thin and regular, the black ground is even, and the dragon's scales shift through three greens, a blue, and a touch of rust-red without bleeding across boundaries.

![Cloisonné box open showing cobalt interior](/assets/images/cloisonne-dragon-box-2.jpg)

The real surprise is inside. Lift the lid and the interior glows with a saturated cobalt blue enamel that has nothing to do with the exterior palette. This is not accidental. Chinese cloisonné workshops have long used interior enamel as a quality marker: cheap tourist pieces leave the inside raw metal or slap on a thin, milky wash. A full interior coat in a contrasting colour signals that the maker cared enough to finish what nobody would see unless they opened it. The blue here is slightly uneven—thicker at the base, thinner at the rim—which suggests hand-dipping rather than machine spraying. That imperfection is, paradoxically, reassuring.

![Cloisonné box lid with scale pattern](/assets/images/cloisonne-dragon-box-3.jpg)

The lid carries its own programme. A coiled creature—dragon, qilin, or something between the two—occupies the centre, ringed by a border of green and white fish-scale cells. The scale motif is mechanically precise, almost certainly stamped or die-cut rather than individually wired, which places this piece in the mid-20th-century export stream rather than the earlier atelier tradition. Below the rim, a band of clouds separates the lid from the body, creating a visual joint that disguises the seam when closed.

![Cloisonné box standing closed](/assets/images/cloisonne-dragon-box-4.jpg)

Attribution is tricky. There is no mark, no chop, no factory stamp on the base. The colour palette—black ground, bright primaries, gold rims—matches the output of several Beijing-area workshops that supplied the export market from the 1950s through the 1980s. The wire is copper, not silver, which rules out the pre-war prestige ateliers. The gilding on the rim is thin and already showing wear at the high points, another indicator of mid-century production quantities rather than imperial commissioning.

![Cloisonné box open from above](/assets/images/cloisonne-dragon-box-5.jpg)

What to do with an object like this? It is too small for jewellery, too decorative for pills. The collecting world for Chinese cloisonné has always been bifurcated: serious money chases large vessels from the Qianlong period or the early Republic, while small lidded boxes, spoon trays, and ashtrays languish in the £20–£80 bracket at auction. But there is a quieter pleasure in owning the minor pieces. They were made in volume, yes, but by hands that still understood the grammar of the form. The dragon on this box is not a museum masterpiece. It is a competent, confident piece of craft, hiding a secret blue room inside, waiting for someone to lift the lid.

*Provenance unknown. Likely Beijing-area workshop, mid-20th century.*
