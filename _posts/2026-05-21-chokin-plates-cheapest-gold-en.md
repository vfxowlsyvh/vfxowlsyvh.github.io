I trade gold. Not physically — I do not handle bars or coins or keep vaults. I watch the price move on a screen, buy and sell contracts, and measure my position in ounces that exist as ledger entries rather than metal. This is how most modern gold investors experience the metal: as a number, a colour on a chart, a ratio against dollars. The actual substance is abstract. I know what gold is worth. I rarely see what gold looks like.

![Close-up showing gold foil inlay on temple scene](/assets/images/chokin-plates-batch3-2.jpg)

These four plates fix that. They are Chokin — Japanese engraved metalwork with gold and silver foil applied to brass or copper bases — and they cost between two and five pounds each in the British second-hand market. For less than the price of a lunch, I can hold a disk of metal that carries genuine gold leaf, visible to the eye, catching light in the specific way that only gold catches light. Not paint. Not plastic with metallic pigment. Actual gold, beaten thin and pressed into engraved grooves by someone who understood that the value of the material is partly in its visibility.

![Detail of landscape plate with gold mountain highlights](/assets/images/chokin-plates-batch3-3.jpg)

The contrast with contemporary consumer culture is absurd. A golden Labubu keyring — a plastic toy sprayed with metallic paint — currently sells in European markets for forty to sixty pounds. It contains no precious metal. The colour will flake. The object itself is a disposable collectible designed to be replaced when the next trend arrives. Yet people queue for it. They photograph it. They display it as a status object. Meanwhile, these plates, which contain real gold foil on copper, which have survived decades without tarnishing because the metal is genuine, which depict temples and ships and landscapes with a patience that no injection-moulded toy can replicate, sit in cardboard boxes in charity shops priced lower than a sandwich.

![Fourth plate showing architectural gold detailing](/assets/images/chokin-plates-batch3-4.jpg)

The market is not wrong, exactly. It is simply looking at a different variable. The Labubu keyring is expensive because it is scarce in the moment, because demand is manufactured by marketing, because ownership signals participation in a contemporary tribe. The Chokin plate is cheap because it is abundant in the second-hand stream, because the tribe that valued it has aged out of the market, because its scarcity is historical rather than hype-driven. But if you are interested in gold as a material rather than gold as a narrative, the plate is the rational choice. It is the only object in this price bracket that gives you the sensory experience of gold — the warm reflectivity, the softness of the foil against light, the way it changes from yellow to amber depending on the angle — without requiring you to buy a coin, a bar, or a piece of jewellery.

There is a philosophical point here about abstraction. Modern finance has separated value from material. A gold contract is valuable because the market agrees it is valuable, not because the holder can see or touch the underlying metal. The contract is pure information. The Chokin plate is pure material. It has no ticker symbol. It does not hedge against inflation in any calculable way. It simply exists as a thing that contains gold, that shows gold, that lets you look at gold while you eat breakfast. For a gold investor, that physicality is oddly grounding. It reminds you that the number on your screen corresponds to a substance that someone, somewhere, once thought beautiful enough to beat into foil and press into metal.

The plates will never appreciate in the way that gold bullion might. They are not an investment. They are a viewing device — the cheapest periscope into the physical world that the financial abstraction obscures. You cannot trade them easily. You cannot collateralise them. But you can hold them up to the window and watch the gold catch the morning light. That is worth more than two pounds. It is worth more than most of the things I have bought with gold profits.

*Four Chokin plates. Brass or copper base with hand-engraved gold and silver foil inlay. Subjects include Japanese temples, landscapes, and maritime scenes. Acquired from UK second-hand markets. Price range: two to five pounds each. Genuine gold content: visible but unquantified.*
