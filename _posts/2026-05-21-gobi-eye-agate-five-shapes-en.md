![Five Gobi eye agates laid out: foam, moon, planet, tooth, and badge](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-shape-1.jpg)

Five stones, five impersonations. Gobi agate collectors do not merely hunt for beauty. They hunt for recognition — that jolt of seeing something familiar in mineral form. These five specimens arrived together, and each one has chosen a different disguise.

The first is **foam on beer**. An oval pebble, cream and amber, its surface pitted with bubbles that look exactly like the head on a freshly poured pint. The resemblance is uncanny: the same irregular cell structure, the same gradient from dense froth at the edge to larger, thinner bubbles in the center. Except this foam solidified 150 million years ago and will never collapse.

![Close-up of the beer-foam agate and the crescent moon agate](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-shape-2.jpg)

Next to it sits a **crescent moon** — not the smooth arc of a cartoon, but the pocked, cratered surface of the real thing. Red-ochre chalcedony, scarred by wind and sand, with two concentric eyes embedded in the curve like lunar seas. Turn it over and the back is raw desert varnish, the matte skin that only decades of Gobi sandblasting can produce.

The round stone in the center is a **planet** or a **coin** — depending on your mood. Dark umber chalcedony with white and rust orbital rings, plus a few stray eye formations that drift across the surface like satellites. Stand it on edge and it becomes a sphere, a world in miniature. The banding is continuous, wrapping around the circumference without a break, proof that the shape is original, not carved.

![The round planet agate standing upright](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-shape-3.jpg)

The **tooth** is the strangest. A dagger of green and white banded agate, tapering to a point sharp enough to suggest function. It looks like a shark's tooth or a primitive blade. The banding runs lengthwise, following the taper, which is geologically perverse — agate banding usually conforms to cavity walls, and cavities are rarely this aggressively tapered. Something forced this stone into this shape: perhaps a fracture that filled incrementally, each new layer narrower than the last, until the cavity sealed at the tip.

![The green tooth agate standing among the others](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-shape-4.jpg)

Finally, the **badge**. Peach and coral chalcedony, covered in concentric white circles like a military insignia or a target. But the real feature is at the bottom: a natural perforation, a hole punched through by erosion or by a weaker mineral inclusion that dissolved away. Thread a cord through it and it becomes wearable immediately. No drilling, no alteration. The Gobi itself provided the bail.

![The badge agate with natural perforation, ready to wear](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-shape-5.jpg)

Collectors call this *xing* — shape, the first of the five virtues. But *xing* is not merely symmetry or polish. It is the stone's ability to trigger pattern recognition in the human brain. A stone that looks like nothing is a mineral specimen. A stone that looks like something is a character, an actor, a story. These five are not the best agates I own. They are not the largest or the most vividly banded. But they are the most talkative. Each one insists on being something other than stone.

The market understands this. A plain banded agate might sell for its weight in curiosity. A banded agate that looks like a recognizable object sells for whatever the buyer's imagination will pay. The foam, the moon, the tooth, the badge — these are not categories in mineralogy textbooks. They are categories in the psychology of collectors. The Gobi desert does not set out to make beer foam or shark teeth. It makes voids, fills them with silica, and waits. The rest is our invention.
