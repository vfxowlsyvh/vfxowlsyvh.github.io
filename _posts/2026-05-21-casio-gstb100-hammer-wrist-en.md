![Casio GST-B100 front face showing the large case and orange strap](/assets/images/casio-gstb100-orange-1.jpg)

This is the largest watch I own. It is probably the largest watch I have ever seen on a human wrist. The case diameter is fifty-eight millimeters. The thickness is fourteen and a half. The lug-to-lug distance exceeds the width of some people's forearms. Casio calls it the GST-B100, part of the G-Steel line, and markets it as an executive G-Shock for people who wear suits. In reality, it is a solar-powered billboard that happens to display the time. On a sunny winter day, wrapped in a down jacket and a scarf, you can still see it from fifty meters away. The orange glow leaks out from under the sleeve like a warning light.

![Side angle showing case thickness and strap profile](/assets/images/casio-gstb100-orange-2.jpg)

I replaced the original steel bracelet with an orange resin strap. The factory bracelet was heavy, cold, and added enough mass that the watch felt like a handcuff. The orange strap does not make it subtle — nothing could — but it does make it wearable. It also reduces the weight by approximately forty grams, which is the difference between noticing your watch and constantly noticing your watch. The colour matches nothing in my wardrobe, which is the point. A watch this size does not coordinate. It announces.

The movement inside is Casio’s Tough Solar module, charged by any light source through the dial. In winter, with weak sunlight and short days, the power indicator still reads high. The battery is a rechargeable cell that Casio claims lasts ten months in total darkness after a full charge. I have not tested the darkness claim. I have tested the winter claim, and it holds. A mechanical watch, left under a coat sleeve all day, loses accuracy from temperature swings and lack of motion. The GST-B100 does not care. It sits in the cold, under fabric, collecting whatever photons leak through, and keeps time to within fifteen seconds per month.

![Detail of the dial and carbon fiber insert](/assets/images/casio-gstb100-orange-3.jpg)

Casio’s marketing team ran a truck over this watch. It survived. They dropped it from a helicopter. It survived. They froze it, boiled it, and vibrated it at frequencies that would shake a building apart. It survived. These tests are real, and the engineering behind them is real — carbon core guard structure, layered resin and metal case, shock-absorbing gel around the module. But the tests are also theatre. No one buys a GST-B100 because they need a watch that can survive a truck. They buy it because they want to believe their watch could survive a truck. That belief is worth more than the engineering.

There is a Bluetooth connection that syncs the time with your phone. I have used it twice. Once to set the watch when I bought it, and once to show someone that the watch had Bluetooth. It is not a feature. It is a justification. It allows Casio to charge more than the basic G-Shock models by adding a layer of digital sophistication to what is essentially the same quartz module. The phone app also tracks the charge level and the number of times the watch has received light, which is information no one has ever needed.

![Wrist shot showing scale against skin and sleeve](/assets/images/casio-gstb100-orange-4.jpg)

The dial is busy. Three sub-dials display the day, the mode, and the charge level. The main hands are skeletonized and coated in luminescence that fades faster than you would like. There is a date window at four o’clock, tilted slightly, as if the designer ran out of room and improvised. The bezel is brushed steel with exposed screws, giving the whole thing an industrial aesthetic that sits somewhere between a luxury sports watch and a piece of construction equipment. It is not beautiful in the classical sense. It is impressive in the architectural sense. You do not admire it. You acknowledge it.

Wearing the GST-B100 changes your posture. The weight pulls your left arm down slightly. The size prevents shirts from sliding over it easily. You become conscious of doorframes and table edges in a way that smaller watches do not require. These are not complaints. They are the terms of the contract. You wear this watch because you want to feel it. You want the occasional clunk against a desk to remind you that you have chosen to carry a small machine on your wrist. If you wanted to forget about your watch, you would wear an F-91W.

Casio makes watches at every scale of existence. The F-91W is a digital whisper. The GST-B100 is a mechanical shout. Both are correct. Both are Casio. The difference is whether you want to disappear or be seen from fifty meters away, through a winter coat, on a cloudy day. I own both. The choice depends on the weather.

*Casio GST-B100. Tough Solar quartz movement. 58mm case diameter. Carbon core guard. Bluetooth time sync. 200m water resistance. Orange resin strap (aftermarket replacement for factory steel bracelet).*
