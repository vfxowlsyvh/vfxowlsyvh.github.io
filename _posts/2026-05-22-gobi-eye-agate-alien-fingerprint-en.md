![Gobi eye agate showing side relief and concentric eye patterns](/assets/images/gobi-alien-1.jpg)

This stone is slightly larger than a twenty-pence coin. Hold it in your hand and it disappears, a flat pebble that weighs almost nothing. Point a camera at it and it expands. The lens finds circles within circles, rings so fine they seem drawn with a single hair, arranged in patterns that no human hand could have planned. I call it **the alien's fingerprint** — not because I believe in extraterrestrials, but because the regularity of these concentric eyes exceeds anything I have seen on a Gobi agate before. They look too deliberate. Too precise. Too much like a coded signature.

![Top-down view of Gobi eye agate with twenty pence coin for scale](/assets/images/gobi-alien-2.jpg)

The surface carries multiple scales of organisation. At the largest scale, there are half a dozen major eyes — concentric bullseyes with raised centres that protrude from the surface like low domes. Between these major eyes, smaller eyes cluster in groups of three and four, each with its own set of rings. Then, filling the gaps, there are pinprick circles — hundreds of them — barely a millimetre across, each one still perfectly circular, still banded, still readable as an eye. The finest circles on this stone are finer than the ridges on a human fingerprint. And unlike a fingerprint, which is chaotic, these circles obey a geometry. They expand, overlap, and interlock like ripples in a pond that has been disturbed at a hundred points simultaneously.

![Top-down view showing dense concentric eye patterns across the agate surface](/assets/images/gobi-alien-3.jpg)

The colour is layered. A dark olive base provides the background. Over this, bands of grey-blue, sea-green, and chalk-white alternate in sequences that vary from eye to eye. Some eyes have tight, rapid banding — twenty rings in the space of a fingernail. Others spread their bands widely, creating靶心-like targets with calm, open centres. The colours do not bleed into each other. Each band is a discrete event, a separate episode of silica deposition, separated from its neighbours by a chemical shift that occurred thousands or tens of thousands of years apart. The stone is a calendar written in chemistry.

![Another angle showing the complexity of overlapping eye formations](/assets/images/gobi-alien-4.jpg)

What makes this agate singular is the **basso relievo** quality. The eyes are not flat decorations painted on the surface. They are physical elevations. Run your thumb across the stone and you feel the major eyes rise and fall — gentle bumps where the concentric rings have built up thicker deposits of chalcedony. The minor eyes create subtler undulations. Even the pinprick circles, under magnification, show micro-topography. It is as if the stone were a relief map of an alien world, with each eye representing a mountain, a crater, a city.

![Top view showing the other face of the agate with contrasting patterns](/assets/images/gobi-alien-5.jpg)

Flip it over and the story continues. The reverse face shows different patterns — more scattered, less densely packed, with a rougher patch where the original volcanic vesicle wall was exposed before silica filled the cavity. The two faces do not match. They are not supposed to. Each side records a different moment in the stone's formation, a different angle of groundwater seepage, a different set of chemical conditions. The agate is not a sphere with symmetry. It is a fragment of a larger story, broken from its matrix by desert wind millions of years after the last ring was laid down.

The collecting value of a stone like this is not in its size. It is in its information density. A large, plain agate is a blank. This small stone contains more visual data than most paintings. Every square millimetre carries a unique pattern. Zoom in with a macro lens and new structures emerge: fractures healed by later silica, tiny bubbles trapped between bands, colour shifts that suggest changes in the water chemistry — perhaps a drought, perhaps a flood, perhaps a volcanic eruption that altered the local groundwater composition. The stone is a geological hard drive. The fingerprint is just the file system.

*Gobi eye agate. Approximately 5 cm across. Multiple scales of concentric eye formations from major domes to pinprick circles. Basso relievo surface topography. Formed 150–300 million years ago in volcanic vesicles, polished by Gobi desert wind.*
