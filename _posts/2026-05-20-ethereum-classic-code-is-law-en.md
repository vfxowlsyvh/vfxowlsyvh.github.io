On 17 June 2016, an attacker exploited a flaw in The DAO—a decentralised investment fund built on Ethereum—and drained roughly $60 million worth of ether. The Ethereum community faced a choice: roll back the blockchain to return the stolen funds, or accept the theft as an immutable fact of a system governed by code. The majority chose the rollback. A minority said no. That minority became **Ethereum Classic (ETC)**.

The fork happened on 20 July 2016 at block 1,920,000. Ethereum Classic preserved the original chain, including the attacker's transactions, under the banner of *"code is law."* Immutability was not a bug to be fixed; it was the entire point. If a blockchain could be rewritten by social consensus whenever something went wrong, its supporters argued, then it was no more trustworthy than a traditional bank ledger with a compliant database administrator.

Nine years later, that philosophical stubbornness has become a study in marginalisation. When Ethereum transitioned to Proof-of-Stake in September 2022—an event known as The Merge—Ethereum Classic stayed behind as the last major smart-contract platform running on Proof-of-Work. GPU miners who had secured Ethereum for years migrated en masse to ETC, briefly boosting its hashrate to record levels. But mining power does not guarantee relevance. Without the developer activity, decentralised finance protocols, and NFT marketplaces that followed Ethereum post-fork, ETC became something closer to a museum piece: a functioning blockchain that few actually use.

The security record has not helped. Ethereum Classic suffered multiple 51% attacks in 2020, at one point requiring exchanges to demand hundreds of block confirmations before accepting deposits. The attacks were possible because ETC's hashrate, even after the post-Merge influx, remained a fraction of what Ethereum had commanded. A blockchain that prides itself on immutability looks rather less principled when attackers can rewrite recent history with rented mining rigs.

And yet, ETC persists. It maintains a fixed supply cap—approximately 210 million coins, unlike Ethereum's uncapped issuance—giving it a Bitcoin-like scarcity narrative. It still processes transactions. It still runs smart contracts, even if the ecosystem is sparse. And it still attracts a small cadre of believers who view the chain as the "honest" Ethereum: the one that did not compromise its principles for convenience.

There is a certain poetry to the position. Ethereum Classic is a living fossil, a snapshot of what Ethereum was in 2016 preserved in amber. Its blocks continue to tick forward, indifferent to the fact that the world moved on. The DAO hacker's wallet still sits on the chain, untouched, a permanent monument to the decision not to intervene. In an industry obsessed with progress, upgrades, and pivoting, Ethereum Classic's refusal to change is itself a kind of statement.

Whether that statement is worth anything in practical terms is the unanswered question. The market has voted with its capital: Ethereum's market capitalisation dwarfs ETC by two orders of magnitude. The developer community has voted with its code: nearly every significant decentralised application chose the forked chain. And the mining community, after its initial post-Merge migration, has largely drifted to other algorithms or capitulated to the economics of Proof-of-Stake.

Ethereum Classic exists, then, as a philosophical control group. It is the Ethereum that did not fork, did not switch consensus mechanisms, and did not chase scalability at the cost of decentralisation. It is the chain that refused to bend. Whether rigidity is a virtue or a death sentence depends entirely on what you believe a blockchain is for.
