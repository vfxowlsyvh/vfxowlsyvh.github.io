## The Computer Spec Test

When you buy a laptop, you do not pay ten times more because a tennis player endorsed it. You check the processor, the RAM, the screen resolution, the build material, and the battery capacity. Each spec has a known market rate. You know what you are paying for.

Watches should work the same way. Sapphire crystal. Stainless steel case. Water resistance rating. Movement type and accuracy. Bezel material. Lume compound. Each of these has a cost. Each has a performance number. And yet, for decades, the watch industry convinced buyers to ignore the spec sheet and buy the story instead.

## The Make-Up

For two generations, the luxury watch business ran on information asymmetry. "904L steel" sounded like aerospace engineering. "Superlative Chronometer" sounded like a laboratory certification. "Sapphire crystal" sounded exotic enough to justify a four-figure premium. Most buyers could not verify these claims. They bought the magazine ad, the airline lounge poster, the country club wrist. They bought a signal in a world where signals were scarce and expensive to authenticate.

The business model was elegant: take a competent industrial product, apply narrative make-up, restrict supply through artificial waiting lists, and charge ten to twenty times the manufacturing cost. It worked because the buyer had no easy way to look under the hood. A Rolex was expensive because it was expensive. The price was the product.

## The Internet Removes the Veil

That world is gone. In the last fifteen years, the internet did to watches what it did to every other opaque market: it turned buyers into auditors.

Today, a prospective buyer can watch a factory tour of a Shenzhen workshop and see the same CNC machines, the same oils, the same QC jigs that supply parts to Swiss brands. They can watch a YouTube teardown of a $200 Sea-Gull and a $8,000 Omega and see that both use the same generic movement architecture. They can read macro photographs of finishing and learn to distinguish Geneva stripes from printed patterns. They can compare water resistance tests, lume graphs, and accuracy reports posted by thousands of anonymous owners on Reddit and WatchUSeek.

The waiting list trick is equally exposed. When Chrono24 lists twenty thousand Submariners and forum veterans explain that the "scarcity" is a dealer allocation game, the trick becomes insulting rather than enticing. New money does not queue. It reads the spec sheet.

## The Three Tiers

In a transparent market, watchmaking separates into three distinct tiers.

**Top tier: True craft.** Patek Philippe, Philippe Dufour, F.P. Journe, MB&F, Greubel Forsey, Rexhep Rexhepi. These are not brands in the marketing sense. They are ateliers. Their value lives in mechanical inventions that cannot be reverse-engineered into a spreadsheet — Dufour's hand-finishing, Journe's resonance chronometry, Rexhepi's philosophical approach to time, MB&F's sculptural mechanics. Information transparency does not hurt them. It validates them. The more you know about watchmaking, the more you understand why a Simplicity costs what it costs. They are thriving.

**Bottom and middle tier: Honest manufacture.** Sea-Gull, San Martin, the workshops of Dongguan and Shenzhen, Japanese microbrands, and Kickstarter projects like Halcyon. These thrive because the barrier to entry collapsed. A good CNC machine, a reliable Sea-Gull or Miyota movement supply chain, and competent quality control are now sufficient to make a watch that objectively meets or exceeds the specs of a $5,000 Swiss piece at one-tenth the price. They do not pretend to be Patek. They do not need to. They are thriving.

**The squeezed middle: The giants.** Rolex, Omega, Cartier, Breitling, TAG Heuer. These built empires on the assumption that buyers could not evaluate what they were buying. Their value proposition was *expensive competence* — competent industrial products wrapped in narrative make-up. In a transparent market, that is the worst place to be. They are neither exceptional (top tier) nor honest (bottom tier). They are not getting very interesting nowadays.

## The Collector's Stance

To collect in this environment is to make a rational choice. You are not buying Chinese watches because you cannot afford better. You are buying them because you have read the spec sheet, watched the teardown, compared the macro shots, and you know exactly what you are getting. You know that a $200 Chinese diver with a sapphire crystal, ceramic bezel, and 200-meter water resistance is the same functional object as a $5,000 Swiss diver with the same specs, minus the logo and the waiting list theatre.

The vintage pieces in a collection — a Soviet Luch, a father's Enicar, a 1970s Montine — are reminders that make-up washes off. Those brands had status once. They had marketing, prestige, state patronage. Now they are metal, memory, and condition. The same will happen, selectively, to today's over-marketed giants. The only question is whether you are a keeper or a trader when the make-up comes off.

## Conclusion

The "everybody can make a watch" market is real. It is not a race to the bottom. It is a market correction. The top will remain the top because true craft is scarce. The bottom will rise because honest manufacture is now global. And the middle — the marketing middle — will have to learn to compete on specs, or watch its buyers walk to AliExpress and Kickstarter with their calculators open.
