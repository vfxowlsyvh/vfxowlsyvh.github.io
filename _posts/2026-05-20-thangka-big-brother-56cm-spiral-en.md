![Close-up of the red HRIH seed syllable at the centre](/assets/images/thangka-large-hrih-1.jpg)

There is a smaller one. Thirty-three centimetres, black ground, gold text, an Om at the centre. This is its sibling: fifty-six centimetres of the same idea, scaled up until the spiral of text becomes physically disorienting. Stand too close and the circles seem to rotate. Step back and the whole thing snaps into a single mandala, flat and watchful. The only difference, besides size, is the seed syllable at the nucleus. The little one has Om. This one has **Hrih**—red, jagged, unmistakably the sound of Amitabha and his earthly delegate, Avalokiteshvara.

![Angled view showing the depth of the text spirals](/assets/images/thangka-large-hrih-2.jpg)

Scale does something strange to repetition. The smaller thangka reads as a decorated object. You look at it, you note the craftsmanship, you move on. At fifty-six centimetres, the mantra stops being decoration and starts becoming architecture. The concentric rings are no longer tidy borders; they are floors of a building you could theoretically walk down, each level a narrower corridor of gold script. The eye travels inward compulsively, hunting the red syllable like a target. It is the visual equivalent of counting breaths: supposedly calming, occasionally hypnotic, sometimes mildly vertiginous.

![Full mandala showing density of text rings](/assets/images/thangka-large-hrih-3.jpg)

The framer made odd choices. Green bars at top and bottom, black matting, a white outer frame. It looks like a corporate certificate that has undergone a spiritual awakening. Traditionalists would wince. But the green does something useful: it anchors the blue outer ring of the mandala, preventing the black centre from bleeding into endless void. Without those green bars, the thing would float. With them, it sits. It becomes furniture. And that, presumably, is the point. This is not a thangka waiting for a consecration ceremony. It is a thangka that has already been consecrated by the act of being hung in a hallway and walked past every morning.

![Full view in frame with green borders](/assets/images/thangka-large-hrih-4.jpg)

Owning two sizes of the same mantra raises a question neither art history nor Buddhism answers satisfactorily: why? Perhaps the first one was a test. Can I live with this much gold script on black? Apparently yes. So the second one arrives, larger, more insistent. Or perhaps the logic is simpler. The small one is for looking at. The big one is for being swallowed by. One is an object. The other is an environment.

There is a moment, usually late afternoon, when the light hits the gold at a shallow angle and the text ignites. The smaller thangka does this too, but the effect is polite—a glint, a nod. The big one flares. Entire rings of syllables vanish into pure light while others remain dark, creating a flickering moiré that makes the static surface feel alive. For a few seconds, before the sun moves, you understand why monks spent months painting these: not because the deity requires it, but because the human eye does. We are wired to chase glowing spirals toward a red centre. It is older than Buddhism. It is probably older than language.

The Hrih syllable itself is worth a second glance. In Siddham script, it is the phonetic seed of compassion—the compressed sonic DNA of a bodhisattva who vowed not to attain final enlightenment until every sentient being was freed from suffering. That is a heavy payload for a single red character barely two centimetres tall. But then, all mantras operate on the same principle: infinite meaning, minimal syllables, repeated until the repetition becomes the meaning.

I do not know how many times Om Mani Padme Hum is written here. Hundreds. Thousands, maybe, if you count the partial repetitions clipped by the circular format. The number does not matter. What matters is that someone, somewhere, sat for days with a brush loaded with gold paint and wrote the same six syllables over and over until a black circle became a starfield. Then it was shipped across an ocean, framed in green and white, and hung on a wall where nobody chants it, nobody bows to it, and nobody knows exactly where it came from. It just radiates. That is enough.

*Two thangkas, same format, different scales. Small: 33 cm, centre Om. Large: 56 cm, centre Hrih. Both framed, not mounted in brocade.*
