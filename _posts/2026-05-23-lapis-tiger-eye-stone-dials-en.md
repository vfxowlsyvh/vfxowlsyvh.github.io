Two more builds. Both use Seiko NH35 movements, both were assembled from AliExpress parts, and both carry dials made from actual stone — not paint, not lacquer, not a printed texture. One is lapis lazuli. The other is tiger's eye. In a factory watch, either dial material would push the price into four or five figures. Here the total spend on each is well below three.

---

**Lapis Lazuli**

![Lapis lazuli dial close-up](/assets/images/lapis-tiger-watches-3.jpg)

The case is a Datejust homage: 36 mm, fluted bezel, cyclops magnifier over the date window. The dial is a slice of lapis lazuli, the deep blue metamorphic rock flecked with pyrite that catches light like stars in a night sky. The gold baton hands and the fluted bezel pick up the pyrite tones. The cyclops is functional — the date is readable — but it also breaks the dial surface just enough to remind you this is a watch, not a jewellery box interior.

![Lapis lazuli dial with date](/assets/images/lapis-tiger-watches-4.jpg)

The caseback is display, which on a stone-dial watch is an interesting choice. Normally the dial is the only thing you want to look at. But the NH35 is visible here, and there is a quiet satisfaction in knowing the mechanical heart behind the mineral face. The rotor carries the Seiko branding — a reminder that the movement inside is the same calibre found in watches ten times the price.

---

**Tiger's Eye**

![Tiger's eye dial close-up](/assets/images/lapis-tiger-watches-5.jpg)

The case here is different — a dive-style bezel with black aluminium insert, coin edge, and a heavier presence on the wrist. The dial is tiger's eye, the chatoyant quartz that shimmers in bands of honey and amber as it catches the light. The hands are teal, a colour choice that should not work but does. Warm stone and cool hands create a tension that keeps the watch from reading as too traditional. The dive bezel is non-functional in any real sense — this is not a watch for the ocean — but it frames the stone dial with a tool-watch aggression that makes the luxury material feel less formal.

![Tiger's eye dial angled](/assets/images/lapis-tiger-watches-6.jpg)

The date window cuts into the stone at three o'clock, a small violence that reveals the dial is a thin veneer over a metal carrier. That is how stone dials are made — a slice of mineral bonded to a brass disc — but the cut is clean and the chamfer around the window is neat. At this price point, that level of finishing is not guaranteed.

---

**The Straps**

Both watches wear black ostrich leather. The leather is textured with the characteristic quill bumps of the hide, and the contrast stitching is cream. Ostrich is an unusual choice for a build at this level — most modders default to cheap calf or NATO straps — but it makes sense once you wear it. The leather is light, surprisingly water-resistant, and so thin and soft that it nearly disappears on the wrist. Typing at a keyboard, you forget it is there. That is the point. A heavy watch with a heavy strap becomes a chore. A heavy watch with a light strap becomes wearable.

![Display casebacks showing NH35 movements](/assets/images/lapis-tiger-watches-2.jpg)

---

**Design Notes**

The two watches make an interesting pair because they approach the same idea — a stone dial on a budget — from opposite directions. The lapis build goes dressy: fluted bezel, gold hands, classic proportions. It asks to be worn with a shirt cuff. The tiger's eye build goes sporty: dive bezel, teal hands, tool-watch case. It does not care what you are wearing. Both are slightly wrong in theory — stone dials belong on precious metal cases with matching bracelets, not on stainless steel with leather — but that wrongness is what makes them interesting. They are luxe materials in utilitarian packaging. The lapis does not know it is in a sub-£200 watch. The tiger's eye does not know it is pretending to be a diver.

That is the pleasure of modding. You are not buying a brand's story. You are writing your own, one component at a time, and the result is a watch that exists nowhere else.
