Bill Gates owns the black one. He has been photographed wearing it at barbecues, in meetings, and on stages where he talks about eradicating malaria. It is a forty-seven-millimeter resin diver with a quartz module, a unidirectional bezel, and a day-date window at three o'clock. The retail price is approximately twenty dollars. I own the orange version, and I use it for rock climbing. Not because it is a climbing watch — it has no altimeter, no barometer, no GPS — but because it is light enough to forget and legible enough to read with one hand while the other is jammed into a crack.

![Side view showing bezel and case thickness](/assets/images/casio-mrw200h-orange-2.jpg)

The MRW-200H is part of Casio's budget diver line, which exists in a strange category between toy and tool. The case is resin. The strap is resin. The crystal is mineral glass. The bezel insert is painted aluminium. The movement is a basic quartz module with a three-year battery life. None of these materials are premium. None of them are trying to be. What the watch offers is information density at a glance: large Arabic numerals, a clear day-date window, a bezel that can track elapsed time, and a weight so low it does not affect your swing or your balance. On a multi-pitch route, where every gram matters and you cannot pull out a phone, that combination is more useful than a chronograph certified to a hundredth of a second.

![Angled dial shot showing the orange bezel insert](/assets/images/casio-mrw200h-orange-3.jpg)

The bezel is worth a separate mention. It is one hundred and twenty clicks, unidirectional, with a serrated edge that grips even when wet with sweat or chalk. The orange insert matches the case and fades over time — not uniformly, but in patches, giving each watch a unique patina that develops according to how you abuse it. Mine has a pale spot at the forty-five-minute mark where my thumb rests when I am resting on a ledge, checking how long I have been on the route. That spot is a memory. A more expensive watch would resist the fading, and you would lose the record.

The day-date function is why I chose this over the F-91W for climbing. The F-91W tells time perfectly, but it does not tell you what day it is. On a three-day climbing trip, when you are sleeping in a van and waking before dawn, the boundary between Tuesday and Wednesday dissolves. The MRW-200H restores it. The day wheel is bilingual — English and Spanish on most versions — which is a small courtesy to the global market that Casio includes at no extra charge. You do not need Spanish on a cliff face in Yorkshire. But the watch does not know that, and there is something reassuring about a device that assumes you might.

![Full watch on wrist showing scale and orange strap](/assets/images/casio-mrw200h-orange-4.jpg)

Gates wears his because it is the perfect anti-status symbol. A man worth a hundred billion dollars choosing a twenty-dollar watch sends a specific message: I do not need to signal wealth, and I have better things to think about. I wear mine for the opposite reason. I am not signaling wealth. I am signaling that I do not care if this watch gets scratched against granite, soaked in river water, or dropped from a height that would shatter most automatic movements. The resin case bounces. The quartz module does not care about shock. The battery does not care about temperature. If I destroy it, I will buy another on the way home and set it in the car park.

There is a category of objects that improve as they get cheaper. Rope improves as it gets lighter. Tents improve as they get smaller. Watches, for specific purposes, improve as they get cheaper because the cheaper they are, the less you hesitate to use them for their intended purpose. The MRW-200H is not a watch you save for special occasions. It is a watch you wear when the occasion involves risk, dirt, and the possibility that you will not remember what day it is until you look at your wrist.

*Casio MRW-200H. Quartz movement. 47mm resin case. Unidirectional 120-click bezel. Day-date function. 100m water resistance. Orange resin strap. Approximately twenty dollars.*
