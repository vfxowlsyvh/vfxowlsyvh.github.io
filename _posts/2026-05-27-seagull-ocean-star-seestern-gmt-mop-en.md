Chinese dive watches used to be a single category: cheap Seiko homages, assembled in Shenzhen, sold on AliExpress with suspicious depth ratings. That story is outdated. The current landscape has split into two distinct species — the state-factory flagship and the microbrand chameleon. These two watches sit on opposite ends of that divide.

---

### Sea-Gull Ocean Star: The State Diver

![Sea-Gull Ocean Star black dial close-up](/assets/images/seagull-seestern-3.jpg)

Sea-Gull is not a microbrand. It is the largest mechanical watch movement manufacturer in the world, the descendant of the Tianjin Watch Factory founded in 1955, and the factory that produced China's first wristwatch. The Ocean Star is their flagship diver — 300 metres of water resistance, a ceramic bezel, a sapphire crystal, and a dial that carries the red star logo without irony.

![Angled view showing the ceramic bezel](/assets/images/seagull-seestern-4.jpg)

The dial is a deep black sunburst that shifts to navy under direct light. Applied indices are thick and faceted, filled with luminous compound. The red star at six o'clock is the same symbol that appears on Red Star watches, on Chinese Air Force chronographs, and on the flag — a piece of national iconography dropped casually into a tool-watch dial. The handset is broad and arrow-shaped, designed for legibility at depth rather than elegance at dinner. The date window at six o'clock is unobtrusive, framed but not magnified.

![Ocean Star engraved caseback](/assets/images/seagull-seestern-2.jpg)

The caseback is a solid steel disc engraved with "OCEAN STAR" and a star set inside wave-patterned rings. It is decorative in the way state-factory engravings often are — confident, slightly loud, technically competent rather than refined. The movement inside is a Sea-Gull automatic calibre, likely the ST2130 or a derivative, which is an in-house design that does not need to pretend to be Swiss. It beats at 28,800 vibrations per hour, hacks, hand-winds, and keeps time within the expected tolerances of a mid-tier automatic.

This is a watch made by an institution. It carries the weight of that institution — the history, the scale, the occasional clumsiness of a factory that produces millions of movements a year and still feels the need to put a red star on everything it makes.

---

### Seestern GMT: The Microbrand Chameleon

![Seestern GMT light blue mother-of-pearl dial](/assets/images/seagull-seestern-5.jpg)

Seestern operates in a different universe. It is a microbrand, probably run out of a small office with a website and an AliExpress store, producing watches that wear their influences openly. This GMT model is a clear descendant of the Rolex GMT-Master template — 24-hour bezel, dual-time hand, date window, tool-watch proportions — but with one decisive twist: the dial is light blue mother-of-pearl.

![Angled view showing the MOP dial colour shift](/assets/images/seagull-seestern-6.jpg)

Mother-of-pearl is not a material you expect on a dive watch. It belongs on dress pieces, on ladies' jewellery watches, on delicate things that fear water. Here it is, installed on a 200-metre tool watch with a blue-and-black bezel insert, shimmering between pale turquoise and silver depending on the angle. The GMT hand is a bright blue arrow that stands out against the iridescent background. The effect is strange and slightly disorienting — a utilitarian chassis wrapped in a material that seems to be trying to escape.

The caseback is solid and unremarkable, which is appropriate. Seestern is not trying to sell heritage. It is trying to sell a specific combination of features at a specific price point. The movement is likely a Chinese GMT calibre — possibly the Sea-Gull ST2100 GMT or a similar derivative — which offers genuine dual-time functionality for a fraction of what a Swiss GMT would cost.

![Both casebacks side by side](/assets/images/seagull-seestern-2.jpg)

Where the Ocean Star is declarative — "I am a Chinese dive watch from a famous factory" — the Seestern is evasive. It borrows a Swiss silhouette, adds an unexpected dial material, and avoids branding that would pin it to a single identity. It is a watch for people who want the GMT function without the GMT price, and who do not mind that the dial looks like it was borrowed from a cocktail party.

---

### What They Mean Together

These two watches are not competitors. They are not even really in the same market. The Ocean Star is bought by people who know what Sea-Gull is and want a piece of Chinese industrial history on their wrist. The Seestern is bought by people who want a colourful GMT and do not care which factory assembled it.

But together they show the full range of what Chinese watchmaking is doing right now. On one side, a state-owned giant producing serious tool watches with in-house movements and national symbols on the dial. On the other, a small brand mixing and matching components to create something that no Swiss factory would think to build — a pearl diver, literally, in a world that expects divers to be matte black and serious.

The Ocean Star will outlast the Seestern. It is built heavier, deeper, and with more institutional stubbornness. But the Seestern is more interesting to look at, more likely to start a conversation, and more honest about what it is: a collage, not a monument.

*Sea-Gull Ocean Star: 300m, automatic, ceramic bezel, black sunburst dial, rubber strap. Seestern GMT: 200m, automatic GMT, blue/black bezel, light blue mother-of-pearl dial, leather strap.*
