![Three Gobi eye agates grouped together](/assets/images/gobi-three-stones-1.jpg)

These three stones arrived in a padded envelope from an Etsy seller in Jiangsu Province, China. They had traveled from the Gobi Desert to a warehouse in Lianyungang, then across continents to a desk in the UK. Two are oval, multi-coloured, and veined with impossible layers. The third is unmistakably heart-shaped, and if you turn it one way, it stares back at you with two green eyes. None of this was carved. All of it was accidental.

![The trio arranged with the heart stone below](/assets/images/gobi-three-stones-2.jpg)

**The Heart Stone** sits at the bottom of every photograph, cream-white and slightly translucent, its surface dimpled with botryoidal chalcedony—the grape-like clustering that defines Gobi agate. Two concentric eye formations sit high on the lobes, each with a pale green pupil. Tilt the stone and the "face" appears: two eyes, a suggested nose in the notch between, and the heart's point as a chin. In Chinese collecting culture, figurative stones—those that resemble living things without human alteration—command the highest prices. A 90-gram agate resembling a chick hatching was appraised at 130 million yuan. This heart-faced stone may not reach those auction heights, but the same aesthetic logic applies. Nature did the sculpting.

![Top-down view showing the heart's two eyes](/assets/images/gobi-three-stones-3.jpg)

**The Left Oval** carries a painter's palette. Orange, amber, yellow, and green swirl across its surface like a sunset viewed through acid rain. One massive eye dominates the left flank: a vivid emerald centre ringed by white, grey, and umber bands. The eye protrudes slightly from the surface, a relief formed by differential weathering—softer minerals eroded faster, leaving the harder siliceous rings standing proud. The colours come from ferrous iron and trace minerals absorbed over millions of years. Where the heart stone is subtle and figurative, this one is loud and geological.

![The heart stone's face and the two ovals](/assets/images/gobi-three-stones-4.jpg)

**The Right Oval** is darker, moodier. A reddish-brown body hosts two ghostly white eyes that look almost painted on. The background is a chaos of banding—concentric arcs, cross-cutting veins, and patches of near-black chalcedony. Flip it over and the banding becomes even more complex, a sedimentary record written in silica. This is the most densely patterned of the three, as if multiple geological events competed for the same piece of real estate.

![All three stones with a 20p coin for scale](/assets/images/gobi-three-stones-5.jpg)

The science behind these eyes is stranger than the stones themselves. In 2019, Chinese geologist He Jing prepared thin sections of similar "eye agates" from Alxa (Alashan) in western Inner Mongolia. Under a polarising microscope, he found that the concentric rings are composed of alternating siliceous and ferrocalcite laminae, each 0.1 to 1.0 mm thick. More remarkably, the centres contain microbial laminae with bacterial fiber structures—filamentous cyanobacteria frozen in silica. The eyes may be **stromatolite fossils**: ancient bacterial mats, two billion years old, replaced mineral-by-mineral until only the architecture remained. What looks like a gemstone is, in part, a fossil of life so primitive it predates the oxygenation of Earth's atmosphere.

The Gobi Desert produces these stones through a brutal refinement process. They begin as vesicles in submarine basalt, fill with silica-rich groundwater, and crystallise in layers. Then the ocean vanishes. The Altai Mountains rise. The Tibetan Plateau blocks rain from the Indian Ocean, creating one of the driest climates on Earth. For millions of years, wind and sand blast the exposed stones, rounding them, pitting them, polishing the harder rings while excavating the softer ones. The result is a natural pebble that looks sculpted.

The market for these stones has become genuinely global. Sellers on Etsy and eBay—often based in Lianyungang, Jiangsu, a major Chinese mineral export hub—photograph stones against white backgrounds, list weights in grams, and ship them in bubble mailers to collectors in Europe, North America, and Australia. Prices range from £15 for small specimens to £250+ for large or figurative pieces. The Australian Gemmologist recently published a detailed study of Gobi eye agates, noting their "steely-blue body colour, heavily veined surfaces, and ridged eye formations with yellow outer rings." The paper documented natural shapes including a duckling-formed pebble—proof that the heart-face stone is not an outlier.

There is something quietly unsettling about holding a bacterial fossil that resembles a human face. The heart stone's eyes are not eyes at all. They are the mineralised ghosts of cyanobacteria that photosynthesised when Earth's atmosphere was a methane haze. And yet, across 2 billion years and 150 million years of volcanic and desert transformation, the pattern that emerges is unmistakably biological. We see faces in clouds, in toast, in the grain of wood. But here, the face was already there. We just had to wait for the wind to uncover it.
