![Orange Casio F-91W, face detail](/assets/images/casio-f91w-orange-1.jpg)

Three men wore this watch. One became president of the United States. One became the world’s most wanted terrorist. One may not exist at all. The watch is the same in every photograph: orange resin, three silver buttons, a grey LCD showing the time in block numerals. It weighs twenty grams. It costs less than a pizza. And it has arguably shaped more history than any timepiece made by hand in Switzerland.

![Angled face shot showing the LCD and buttons](/assets/images/casio-f91w-orange-2.jpg)

Ryusuke Moriai designed it in 1989. The case is a rounded rectangle. The strap is a single piece of moulded resin with a black plastic buckle. The screen displays day, date, hour, minute, second, plus an alarm and a stopwatch accurate to a hundredth of a second. Water resistance is stamped below the glass in small letters: WR. Seven-year battery life. No sapphire. No mechanical movement. No heritage. Just a quartz crystal and a circuit board that have not changed in thirty-six years because they did not need to.

The first famous wrist was Barack Obama’s. Photographers caught a black F-91W during his 2007 campaign, peeking from beneath a shirt cuff while he shook hands in Iowa. At the time, commentators treated it as a humble affectation — a politician pretending to be ordinary. They missed the point. The F-91W does not pretend. It simply is ordinary, which is a different and more difficult thing to achieve. You cannot fake ordinariness with a twenty-thousand-dollar watch. You can only achieve it with a ten-dollar one.

The second famous wrist belonged to Osama bin Laden. In multiple videos released between 2001 and 2011, the same black digital watch appears on his left arm. This was not aesthetic preference. A classified CIA document from the Guantánamo interrogations described the F-91W as “the sign of al-Qaeda.” The alarm circuit is simple, reliable, and easily modified. Detonators need timers. Timers need batteries that last and circuits that do not fail. The Casio F-91W became the default choice for improvised explosive devices across three continents not because it was sinister, but because it was trustworthy. The same qualities that made it perfect for a farmer in Laos — cheap, accurate, durable — made it perfect for a bomb maker in the Hindu Kush.

![Diagonal close-up showing the orange strap texture](/assets/images/casio-f91w-orange-3.jpg)

The third wrist is mythical. In the early Bitcoin forums, a rumour circulated that Satoshi Nakamoto wore an orange F-91W while writing the genesis block code in late 2008. The rumour is almost certainly false. The orange variant was not even released until years later. But the story persists because it feels true. Orange is the colour of Bitcoin. The F-91W is the watch of the programmer who does not care about watches. A device that tells time with zero ornamentation is the perfect accessory for a monetary system designed to eliminate ornamentation from finance. Satoshi probably wore nothing on his wrist. But if he had worn something, it would have been this.

![Full vertical view of the watch on white background](/assets/images/casio-f91w-orange-4.jpg)

There is a deeper pattern here. The F-91W is the most democratic object in horology. Presidents and terrorists and coders and grandmothers and schoolchildren all wear it for the same reason: it works, and it costs almost nothing. It is the only watch that makes a Rolex look like jewellery. Not because the Rolex is bad, but because the Casio exposes what a watch actually is. A watch is a circuit or a spring that vibrates at a known frequency, translated into numbers. Everything else — the gold, the heritage, the limited edition — is narrative. The F-91W strips the narrative away until only the function remains. Then, because it is so cheap, it allows you to build your own narrative around it. Obama’s narrative was humility. Bin Laden’s was utility. Satoshi’s, imagined or not, was minimalism.

Casio has sold approximately one hundred million units. The design has not changed because change would be vanity. The resin case yellows slightly after a decade. The buttons lose their click. The battery, eventually, dies. But the watch itself is immortal. You can buy one today in any city on Earth, set it to the exact same time as a watch bought in Tokyo in 1989, and both will drift by less than a second per day. That is not manufacturing. That is architecture.

The orange one is rarer than the black. The black F-91W is invisible — worn by billions of wrists across four decades, noticed by no one. The orange one announces itself. It is the colour of traffic cones, of prison jumpsuits, of Bitcoin. On the wrist, it looks like a joke until you know the history. Then it looks like a secret.

This is the ultimate object. It requires no maintenance, no insurance, no justification. You can lose it and buy another without thinking. You can wear it swimming, sleeping, or building a bomb. It will outlast your phone, your laptop, and probably your government. It is the only watch that has been on the wrist of a Nobel Peace Prize laureate and on the wrist of a man who declared war on the country that gave it to him. And now it is on my wrist. Which feels, somehow, like the correct place for it.

*Casio F-91W, orange resin variant. Designed 1989. Quartz movement. Alarm, chronograph, seven-year battery. Water resistant. Price: approximately ten dollars.*
