![Three additional mushroom agates, darker specimens](/assets/images/gobi-mushroom-agate-12.jpg)

In May 2010, a programmer paid ten thousand bitcoins for two pizzas. At the time, the transaction was ordinary. Bitcoin was new, abundant, and easy to generate on a home computer. The coins were not valuable; they were convenient. Twelve years later, those pizzas became the most expensive meal in history. The story is not about food. It is about the moment when something plentiful and easy to acquire begins its transition into something scarce and impossible to replicate.

![Close-up of grey mushroom showing banded stem texture](/assets/images/gobi-mushroom-agate-13.jpg)

These mushroom agates are in their two-pizza phase. In the rock markets of Mongolia, they are sold by weight, by the sack, by the ton. Herders and traders walk the desert floor, bend down, pick them up, and carry them to market in plastic bags. The transaction is casual. The stones are not appraised individually. They are commodities, like gravel or salt, valued by the kilogram rather than by the specimen. This is possible because the supply appears infinite. The Gobi is vast. The surface is open. The wind continues to expose new material. Why would anyone pay a premium for a stone that costs nothing to extract and requires no skill to find?

![Angled view showing cap and stem proportions](/assets/images/gobi-mushroom-agate-14.jpg)

The answer, as with Bitcoin, is that the easy phase does not last. I did the arithmetic. The Gobi Desert covers approximately one point three million square kilometres. The agate-bearing strata are not continuous; they occupy specific volcanic zones that might cover, at most, one percent of the total surface. That leaves roughly thirteen thousand square kilometres of productive ground. If we assume one mushroom-shaped agate per hundred square metres — a generous estimate that includes broken pieces, buried fragments, and specimens too weathered to recognize — the total inventory is approximately one hundred and thirty million. That sounds like a large number until you distribute it across geological time and human greed.

![Wider shot showing all three specimens with scale](/assets/images/gobi-mushroom-agate-15.jpg)

Human collection of Gobi agates has been happening for thousands of years. Mongolian herders have always picked up interesting stones. Chinese traders have transported them eastward along the same routes that carried silk and tea. In the past two decades, the pace has accelerated: online marketplaces, tourist collectors, and commercial buyers who purchase by the container load. The stones that remain on the surface are the survivors of this continuous harvest. Each one picked up is one fewer left for the future. Unlike a mine, where depth offers the illusion of infinite supply, the Gobi has no depth. It has only surface. And surface is finite.

The difficulty curve is what matters. Bitcoin became harder to mine as the protocol adjusted. The Gobi does not have a protocol. It has erosion, which is slower and less predictable. A new agate is exposed when the wind removes enough sand to uncover it. That process takes years, decades, or centuries depending on the location. The rate of exposure is not keeping pace with the rate of collection. At some point — perhaps already passed in the most accessible regions — the density of collectible specimens will drop below the threshold that makes casual picking worthwhile. The herders will stop bending down. The traders will stop carrying plastic bags. The market will shift from bulk commodity to individual curation.

That shift is the end of the two-pizza phase. It is the moment when a stone that was once traded by the kilogram becomes a stone that is weighed, photographed, and described before it is priced. The mushroom shape, which was previously irrelevant to value, becomes a distinguishing feature. The wind polish, which was previously assumed, becomes a selling point. The continuous banding, which was previously unnoticed, becomes the proof of authenticity that separates natural from carved.

These nine mushrooms — six grey, three orange-red — are not a collection in the traditional sense. They are a position. They represent the act of acquiring assets during the easy phase, before the difficulty adjustment makes casual collection impossible. Whether they become valuable depends not on their intrinsic properties, which have not changed, but on the density of narrative that accumulates around them. Bitcoin became valuable because enough people wrote about it, built infrastructure for it, and argued over its meaning. These stones will become valuable if enough people write about them, photograph them, and argue over their origin.

The Gobi does not care about any of this. It will continue to erode, to expose, to bury, and to forget. The stones are not rare in any absolute sense. What is rare is the attention required to notice them, the knowledge required to understand them, and the patience required to wait for the market to catch up. That is the real scarcity. Not the stones. The observers.
