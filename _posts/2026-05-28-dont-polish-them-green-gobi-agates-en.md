Hold one of these up to a window and light comes through. Not like glass — like a leaf, like a slice of jade, like a peridot that has forgotten it is supposed to be cut. The pale green ones are almost translucent at the edges. The deeper green ones glow. You could polish them. You could put them on a wheel and turn them into cabochons that would not look out of place in a jeweller's tray. But you would be committing a kind of vandalism. The skin tells their story. Polish is erasure.

![The same agates from the reverse angle, with a twenty-pence coin for scale](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agates-green-back.jpg)

---

The green Gobi agates are the most chemically anomalous of the collection. The orange ones have iron. The yellow ones have hydrated iron. The green ones have something else — nickel, chromium, perhaps copper in reduced states — trace elements that should not be common in basaltic host rocks but somehow found their way into these particular cavities. The result is a palette that ranges from deep forest green to pale celadon to almost blue-white, sometimes within a single stone. The small rounded agate at top centre is the most saturated: emerald-green, almost chrysoprase in its intensity. The teardrop beside it is a ghost by comparison, pale green fading to white at the tip, as if the colour ran out of ink.

But what distinguishes these stones is not the colour. It is the surface.

Each agate carries a **desert skin** — the matte, slightly pitted, wind-etched surface that comes from centuries of tumbling in airborne sand. The texture is not uniform. Some areas are smooth as satin where the stone's hardness resisted abrasion. Others are slightly rough, with microscopic pits where softer inclusions were scoured out. The edges are rounded not by design but by statistics: every grain of sand that struck this stone removed an infinitesimal amount of material, and after millions of impacts, geometry surrendered to probability.

This skin is the agate's autobiography. It records where the stone travelled, how long it spent exposed, what kind of wind it endured. A polished agate is a beautiful object with no provenance. A skinned agate is an ugly object with perfect provenance — you know exactly what happened to it because the evidence is still there.

---

There is a strong temptation with translucent stones to polish them. Transparency wants to be shown off. Light wants to pass through unimpeded. A polished chrysoprase cabochon has a glow that a rough stone cannot match. But these are not chrysoprase. They are not peridots waiting to be faceted. They are Gobi agates, and their value is not optical. It is geological and historical.

Polishing would do three things. First, it would remove the desert skin, erasing the only physical evidence of the stone's journey from volcanic cavity to surface to hand. Second, it would homogenise the appearance, turning a unique weathered object into a generic green cabochon indistinguishable from any other. Third, and most importantly, it would impose human time on geological time. The polish is a few minutes of mechanical abrasion. The skin is thousands of years of atmospheric process. Replacing the latter with the former is not improvement. It is substitution of the profound by the trivial.

The round agate at bottom left is the best argument against polishing. It carries dense eye patterns in deep green, the circles standing proud of the surface like relief carving. Polish would flatten these. The eyes would still be visible, but they would lose their tactile dimension — the slight ridge where a harder silica layer resisted erosion while softer material around it yielded. That ridge is data. It tells you that the eye was harder than its surroundings, that the chemistry shifted during formation, that something changed in the groundwater between one layer and the next. A polished surface cannot tell you this. It is too smooth to retain information.

---

The pale green agate at bottom right, near the coin, is almost opalescent — translucent, with a milky quality that diffuses light rather than transmitting it cleanly. This is typical of chalcedony with very fine internal inclusions, probably clay minerals or fluid bubbles trapped during deposition. Polish might make it glow. But the glow would be uniform, decorative, empty. As it is, the stone has variation: some areas are more transparent, some more opaque, creating a mottled surface that catches light differently depending on angle. The desert did this. The desert is a better artist than any lapidary.

There is a category of collector who believes that rough stones are unfinished, that nature produces raw material and humans complete it. This view is common in gemology, where a rough diamond is "just" a crystal and the cut diamond is the artwork. But that hierarchy collapses with objects like these. The Gobi agate is not rough material waiting for a better version of itself. It is a complete object that happens to be unpolished. The wind finished it. Time finished it. There is nothing a human hand can add that improves on that completion.

The deep green agate at bottom centre is the darkest in the set, almost opaque, its surface densely pitted with tiny craters that catch shadow and read as black in low light. This is the most weathered of the batch — the one that travelled farthest, or sat exposed longest, or was caught in a sandstorm dense enough to etch rather than smooth. Its biography is written in Braille. You read it with your fingertips.

---

Collecting these green agates requires a certain discipline. You must resist the jeweller's eye. You must stop seeing potential and start seeing actuality. The stone is not a pre-cabochon. It is a post-journey object, a traveller that has arrived. The skin is not a defect to be removed. It is the only credential that matters — proof that this object passed through time and space in a way that cannot be faked, cannot be replicated, cannot be minted as an NFT or polished into a lie.

Leave them as they are. Hold them to the light if you must. But let the light pass through desert skin, not factory gloss. The glow is dimmer. The truth is brighter.
