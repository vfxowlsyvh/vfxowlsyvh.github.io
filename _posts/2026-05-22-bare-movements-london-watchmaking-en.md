![Collection of antique watch movements and dials, mostly caseless](/assets/images/antique-movements-1.jpg)

These watches have no cases. They are naked movements — mechanical cores stripped of their gold and silver skins, left behind when the precious metal was melted into bars and sold. But they are more than orphaned parts. They are the physical evidence of a lineage that runs from the peak of English watchmaking in the nineteenth century to the independent watchmaking renaissance of today. Hold a J W Benson movement in one hand and a photograph of a Philippe Dufour Simplicity in the other, and the continuity is unmistakable. The same hand-engraved back plates. The same thermally blued screws. The same arrogant belief that a movement should be finished to perfection even where no one will ever see it.

![White enamel dial with Roman numerals and thermally blued steel hands](/assets/images/antique-movements-2.jpg)

**J W Benson** was founded in London in 1847 and grew into one of Britain's most respected makers. The movement here carries the full pedigree: "The Keyless Ludgate Watch / J W Benson / Patent No 4658 / Best London Make / By Warrant to H M The Queen." The white enamel dial was fired at temperatures that would destroy a modern printed dial. The Roman numerals are painted by hand. The hands are thermally blued steel — heated until the surface oxidises to a specific shade of violet-blue, a process that requires judgment and experience rather than a paint code. These were not luxury items in the modern sense. They were the standard output of a healthy craft ecosystem, produced by workshops where hand-finishing was simply how watches were made.

![Ornate enamel dial with gold detailing and floral centre decoration](/assets/images/antique-movements-3.jpg)

The **John Forrest** movement carries a different authority: "Chronometer Maker to the Admiralty." The engraving on the back plate is cut by hand with a burin, each letter formed by a single continuous stroke. The gilt brass surface is frosted, the screw heads are polished and blued, the bridge edges are bevelled and mirrored. In the nineteenth century, this level of finish was expected. In the twenty-first, it is extinct in mass production and survives only among independents.

![J W Benson movement back plate showing engraved pedigree and blued screws](/assets/images/antique-movements-4.jpg)

What killed this tradition was not a lack of skill. It was economics. American watchmaking industrialised first — Waltham and Elgin introduced interchangeable parts and machine production that undercut British handwork on price. Then Switzerland scaled up, consolidating into the brands we know today. The final blow was the quartz crisis of the 1970s, when electronic oscillators rendered mechanical movements obsolete for practical timekeeping. British watchmaking collapsed entirely. The workshops closed. The tools were sold or scrapped. The cases of these beautiful movements were melted for gold, and the mechanical cores — too labour-intensive to be worth saving — were sold by the kilogram to collectors and tinkerers.

![John Forrest movement back plate with Admiralty engraving and hand-cut lettering](/assets/images/antique-movements-5.jpg)

The revival began in 1989, when **Philippe Dufour** launched his own brand in the Vallée de Joux. Dufour had trained in the Swiss industry but rejected its industrial logic. He made everything by hand — plates, bridges, wheels, screws — and finished each component to a standard that exceeded even the best nineteenth-century English work. In interviews, Dufour has cited J W Benson as an inspiration. The connection is not stylistic. It is philosophical. Both believed that the invisible parts of a watch deserve the same attention as the visible ones. Both treated hand-finishing not as decoration but as discipline.

Dufour was not alone. The independent watchmaking movement that gathered momentum in the 1990s and 2000s — F.P. Journe, Kari Voutilainen, Roger W. Smith, and dozens of smaller names — represented a collective rejection of mass production. These makers did not want to design dials for ETA movements. They wanted to build watches from raw metal, the way Benson and Forrest did. The difference was that while the English makers worked in a thriving craft ecosystem, the modern independents work in isolation, often in single-room ateliers, charging prices that reflect the true cost of hand labour.

![Collection of movement backs including J W Benson, John Forrest, and a cased pocket watch](/assets/images/antique-movements-6.jpg)

This is where the collector of bare movements enters the story. These orphaned English movements are textbooks. They show how problems were solved before CNC machines and CAD software. How did you make a lever escapement without wire EDM? How did you harden a mainspring without digital temperature control? The answers are in the metal. Independent watchmakers buy these movements to study, measure, and reverse-engineer. Some collectors 3D-print cases for them, giving old engineering new containers. The practice annoys purists, but the alternative is a drawer full of slowly tarnishing brass.

Wearing one is impractical. The movements are large — forty-four millimetres or more — and loud, with slow balance wheels that tick audibly in quiet rooms. They are inaccurate compared to a quartz watch. But accuracy is not why you own them. You own them because they connect two eras: the workshop of J W Benson, where hand-finishing was standard, and the atelier of Philippe Dufour, who proved that the tradition was worth resurrecting. The gold cases are gone. The thread remains.

*Antique pocket watch movements. English makers including J W Benson (London, 1847–1973) and John Forrest (Chronometer Maker to the Admiralty). Gilt brass movements with hand-engraved back plates, thermally blued steel hands and screws, white enamel dials. Cases missing — presumably melted for precious metal content.*
