![Collection of antique watch movements and dials, mostly caseless](/assets/images/antique-movements-1.jpg)

These watches have no cases. They are naked movements — mechanical cores stripped of their gold and silver skins, left behind when the precious metal was melted into bars and sold. The cases were worth more than the engineering inside them. That is the economics of antiquity. Gold is liquid. Craftsmanship is not. These movements survive because they could not be converted into currency as easily as their containers.

![White enamel dial with Roman numerals and thermally blued steel hands](/assets/images/antique-movements-2.jpg)

The dials are white enamel, fired at temperatures that would destroy a modern printed dial. The Roman numerals are painted by hand, each stroke slightly different from its neighbour. The hands are thermally blued steel — heated until the surface oxidises to a specific shade of violet-blue, a process that requires judgment and experience rather than a paint code. A modern manufacturer would use PVD coating or simply paint the hands. Thermal bluing is slower, riskier, and impossible to replicate exactly. That is why it is rarely done today.

![Ornate enamel dial with gold detailing and floral centre decoration](/assets/images/antique-movements-3.jpg)

Among the movements, **J W Benson** stands out. The back plate is engraved with the full pedigree: "The Keyless Ludgate Watch / J W Benson / Patent No 4658 / Best London Make / By Warrant to H M The Queen." Benson was a London maker of the highest order, and in interviews, the independent watchmaker Philippe Dufour has cited Benson as an inspiration. Dufour, who hand-finishes every component of his Simplicity model, recognised in Benson the same philosophy: that a movement should be beautiful even where no one will see it.

![J W Benson movement back plate showing engraved pedigree and blued screws](/assets/images/antique-movements-4.jpg)

The **John Forrest** movement carries a different authority: "Chronometer Maker to the Admiralty." This was not marketing. The Admiralty required precision for naval navigation, and chronometers that failed testing were rejected outright. The engraving on the back plate is not stamped by machine. It is cut by hand with a burin, each letter formed by a single continuous stroke. The gilt brass surface is frosted, the screw heads are polished and blued, the edges of the bridges are bevelled and mirrored. These finishes served no mechanical purpose. They existed because the maker believed that precision and beauty were the same discipline.

![John Forrest movement back plate with Admiralty engraving and hand-cut lettering](/assets/images/antique-movements-5.jpg)

These movements are educational hardware. Independent watchmakers buy them to study how problems were solved before CNC machines and CAD software. How did you make a lever escapement without a wire EDM? How did you harden and temper a mainspring without digital temperature control? The answers are in the metal. You can take these movements apart, photograph every layer, measure every angle with calipers, and reassemble them. They are open-source engineering from an era before patents meant much and trade secrets were guarded by guilds rather than lawyers.

![Collection of movement backs including J W Benson, John Forrest, and a cased pocket watch](/assets/images/antique-movements-6.jpg)

Some collectors 3D-print cases for these orphaned movements, giving them a second life as wristwatches or pocket watches. The practice is controversial among purists, who argue that a printed case is anachronistic and disrespectful. But the alternative is to leave the movement in a drawer, slowly tarnishing, its enamel dial gathering dust. A printed case at least allows the mechanism to be wound, set, and observed. The movement does not care what contains it. It only cares whether it is allowed to tick.

Wearing one is a different matter. These movements are large — forty-four millimetres or more — and thick, designed for pocket watches rather than wrists. The balance wheels beat slowly, producing a loud, deliberate tick that draws attention in quiet rooms. They are not accurate by modern standards. A cheap Seiko quartz will outperform them by orders of magnitude. But accuracy was never the point. The point is to hold in your hand a machine that was assembled by someone using only hand tools, judgment, and pride, in a workshop that no longer exists, for a customer who is long dead.

The gold cases are gone. The money they fetched was spent generations ago. What remains is the part that could not be liquidated: the intelligence encoded in brass and steel, the patience of hand-finishing, the arrogance of engraving your name on the back of a movement because you believed it would outlast you. It has.

*Antique pocket watch movements. English makers including J W Benson (London, Ludgate Hill) and John Forrest (Chronometer Maker to the Admiralty). Gilt brass movements with hand-engraved back plates, thermally blued steel hands and screws, white enamel dials. Cases missing — presumably melted for precious metal content.*
