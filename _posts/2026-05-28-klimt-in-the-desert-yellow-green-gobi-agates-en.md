![Seven yellow and green Gobi eye agates arranged on wood](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agates-yellow-green-front.jpg)

The orange agates from the previous batch looked like nature's NFTs. These ones look like they have fallen out of a **Gustav Klimt** painting during his golden phase — all decorative circles, metallic yellows, and organic swirls that seem to reference Byzantine mosaics and Art Nouveau at the same time. The rectangular stone second from left carries nested eyes in pale lime and white that read exactly like the ornamental patterns in *The Tree of Life*. The teardrop stone on the far left has a green pupil surrounded by halos of yellow and cream. You half-expect to find gold leaf under the surface.

![The reverse side of the same agates, with a twenty-pence coin for scale](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agates-yellow-green-back.jpg)

But the colour is not paint. It is chemistry. And the chemistry explains why this batch is so different from the orange ones.

---

Agate is essentially pure silica — SiO₂ — deposited in layers from groundwater seeping into volcanic cavities. The base material is colourless. What gives each agate its particular palette is the trace elements that contaminate the silica as it precipitates, and the chemical environment in which that precipitation happens. The orange agates owe their warmth to **iron(III) oxide**, hematite, formed in oxidising conditions where groundwater carried dissolved iron in its ferric state. Red and orange are the default colours of iron-rich agates worldwide.

These yellow and green stones come from a different set of conditions. The bright yellow is **limonite** — hydrated iron oxide, FeO(OH)·nH₂O — chemically similar to the orange hematite but carrying water molecules in its crystal structure. The difference between orange and yellow is often just a question of how much water was present in the groundwater at the time of deposition, and whether the chemical environment was acidic enough to keep the iron in a hydrated form rather than dehydrating to the red oxide. The Gobi is not uniform. Some cavities sat in rock that held more moisture. Some had groundwater with lower pH. Some were simply younger or older deposits, caught at different moments in the desert's hydrological history.

The green is more mysterious. Green chalcedony usually indicates **nickel** or **chromium** substituting into the silica lattice — trace amounts of Ni²⁺ replacing Si⁴⁺ in the crystal structure, or Cr³⁺ doing similar work. These are not common elements in most volcanic settings, which suggests these particular cavities were hosted in rocks with an unusual chemistry — perhaps ultramafic intrusions, or zones where copper-nickel mineralisation had occurred before the agate started forming. Alternatively, the green can come from very fine inclusions of clay minerals or reduced iron compounds trapped between silica layers, producing a pale celadon rather than a vivid emerald.

![Close-up of a teardrop-shaped agate with a green central eye and yellow halos](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-green-teardrop.jpg)

This teardrop agate is the most Klimt-like of the batch. A central green eye — almost jade-coloured — surrounded by concentric rings that shift from white to cream to yellow, then explode into scattered satellite eyes across the upper surface. The pattern is not symmetrical. It is ornamental. Klimt's *Tree of Life* uses the same vocabulary: central medallions, radiating circles, organic borders that refuse to obey geometry. The difference is that Klimt applied gold leaf with a brush. This stone grew its decoration one molecular layer at a time, over millions of years, without ever seeing a sketch.

![Close-up of a rectangular light green agate with multiple nested eyes](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-green-rectangular.jpg)

The rectangular light green stone is stranger. Two large eyes dominate the centre, each with a dark green pupil and pale green iris, set against a background of white and deeper green bands that curve like drapery. The lower portion carries repeating arc patterns — almost architectural, like the frieze on a Secessionist building. The colour is celadon, the tone of Song dynasty ceramics, of weathered copper roofs, of certain jade varieties that collectors call "mutton fat" when pale and "spinach" when dark. There is no single element responsible for this green. It is a coalition: nickel for the hue, perhaps chromium for saturation, trace manganese shifting the tone toward grey, and the yellow-white silica providing the canvas.

![Close-up of a yellow oval agate with dense circular eye patterns](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-yellow-oval.jpg)

The yellow oval is pure limonite colour — warm, metallic, almost mustard in its deeper zones. The eyes here are smaller and more densely packed, creating a pattern like too many targets overlaid on one another. Some eyes are complete circles. Others are sliced by the edge of the nodule, giving the impression of a pattern that continues beyond the visible surface. This is the agate that most directly recalls Klimt's *Portrait of Adele Bloch-Bauer I*: the same density of ornament, the same refusal of empty space, the same sense that decoration has become the subject rather than the frame. Klimt surrounded his sitter with gold and pattern until the woman and the ornament became inseparable. The Gobi has done the same with silica and iron.

![Close-up of a bright yellow rectangular agate with scattered green-eyed circles](/assets/images/gobi-eye-agate-yellow-rectangular.jpg)

The brightest yellow stone in the set is almost rectangular, a rare shape for a wind-tumbled nodule, suggesting it may have started as a more angular fragment before the desert rounded its corners. The surface is saturated — school-bus yellow in the deepest areas, fading to lemon where the silica was purer. Against this field, pale green eyes float like lily pads on a pond. The texture is slightly pitted, matte rather than glossy, because the wind that tumbled it was carrying sand coarse enough to etch the surface rather than polish it. The desert is not a lapidary workshop. It does not aim for perfection. It aims for exposure, and the result is more honest than any buffing wheel.

---

The Klimt comparison is not just aesthetic pretension. There is a genuine structural similarity. Klimt's golden phase works — *The Kiss*, the *Beethoven Frieze*, the *Stoclet Frieze* — replace perspective and modelling with flat planes of gold leaf and decorative pattern. The figure becomes a surface. The surface becomes the content. These agates do the same: there is no depth to read, no internal structure to interpret beyond the pattern itself. The eye moves across the surface the way it moves across a Klimt canvas, finding rhythm and variation without ever penetrating into space.

But where Klimt's patterns were deliberate — every circle placed, every spiral considered — these patterns are the product of groundwater chemistry, temperature fluctuations, and the statistical distribution of trace elements in a Permian volcanic field. The resemblance to human art is a coincidence. The desert has never seen Vienna. It has never heard of Art Nouveau. And yet it produces objects that would not look out of place in the Secession Building.

This is the recurring surprise of collecting Gobi agates. You think you are buying stones. You end up with geology that accidentally imitates art history.

*Gobi Desert eye agates, yellow-green variety. Colour caused by hydrated iron oxides (limonite) and trace nickel/chromium substitutions in chalcedony (SiO₂). Wind-tumbled. Estimated age: 300 million years. Geological provenance: Permian volcanic cavities, Inner Mongolia.*
