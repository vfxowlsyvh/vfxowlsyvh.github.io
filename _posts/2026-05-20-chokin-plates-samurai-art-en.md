![First set of four Chokin plates](/assets/images/chokin-plates-set1.jpg)

Four small porcelain discs, each no larger than a saucer, are shown from two angles. Their centres gleam with etched copper gilded in gold and silver—scenes of a golden skyscraper, a mountain village, flowers with a hummingbird, and koi among water plants, all frozen in metal. A second angle reveals the same four plates under different light: a temple by water, a torii gate before a peak, irises with a butterfly, and a bird over blossoms. The rims are banded in 24-karat gold. Flip them over and the labels tell a story that spans eight centuries: *"The Art of Chokin. The engravings were originally created to decorate the armament of the Samurai warriors. This art form was handed down for over eight hundred years. Made in Japan."*

![Second set of four Chokin plates](/assets/images/chokin-plates-set2.jpg)

These are **Chokin** (彫金) plates—mass-produced decorative items from Japan's 1970s and 1980s export boom, descended from a craft that once adorned the breastplates and helmets of medieval samurai. The technique is genuinely ancient. In the 12th century, Japanese metalworkers developed a method of engraving copper and then overlaying the design with precious metals—gold for nobility, silver for refinement, copper for structure. The results were not merely decorative; they were **intimidating**. A samurai facing battle wanted his armor to announce status, wealth, and ferocity. Chokin delivered all three.

The plates in these photographs represent the domesticated end of that lineage. One disc shows a modern cityscape—a golden skyscraper rising against a silver sky, proof that Chokin subjects evolved with the times. Another captures a mountain village clinging to cliffsides in etched copper. A third view reveals what appears to be Kyoto's Kinkaku-ji temple reflected in water, or perhaps a torii gate framing a snow-capped peak—depending on how the light catches the relief. The same four plates, photographed again from a different angle, show irises with a butterfly, a bird hovering over blossoms, flowers with a hummingbird in gold relief against blackened copper, and koi swimming among water plants. Each plate is porcelain with a recessed metal medallion at its centre and a gold verge separating the art from the rim.

![Backs of the Chokin plates showing labels](/assets/images/chokin-plates-backs.jpg)

The backs reveal the manufacturing context. Some labels carry the full text about samurai warriors and 24-karat gold edging. Others offer a shorter version. All bear the same ambiguous promise: *"The Art of Chokin."* This was not a single studio but a branded export line—plates, vases, bells, clocks, and lamps produced in quantity for Western department stores and gift shops. An appraisal article by Elizabeth Stewart notes that most Chokin items found today date from the mid-20th century, imported by firms in places like Arlington Heights, Illinois. They are the tourist version of a noble medium: acid-etched rather than hand-burined, stamped rather than individually chased.

Does that make them inauthentic? Not exactly. The materials are real—copper, silver, gold. The etching produces genuinely fine detail; the veins on leaves, the scales on koi, the feathers on birds are all crisply rendered. What these plates lack is the **soul** of one-off masterwork. They were made by factories, not by a single artisan hunched over a breastplate for six months. The 20th-century master Risho Arita taught and signed Chokin pieces, but the plates bearing generic labels were produced far from his workshop.

The collector market reflects this reality. Search eBay and you will find Chokin plates listed for anywhere from £8 to £40, depending on size, condition, and whether a display easel is included. They are not investments. They are curios—objects that connect a living room in 2026 to a battlefield in 12th-century Japan through the strange alchemy of global trade.

There is something quietly poignant about that journey. A craft invented to terrify enemies and honour warlords now sits on sideboards, holding nothing more martial than guest soap or spare keys. The samurai who wore Chokin-decorated armour into battle would likely have found the whole thing absurd. But they might also have recognised the underlying impulse: humans have always wanted their possessions to tell stories, to carry meaning beyond function. Whether that meaning is martial glory or domestic beauty is, in the end, just a matter of context.

These plates may be mass-produced, but they are also honest. They do not pretend to be ancient. They do not pretend to be rare. They simply say: *here is a technique that survived eight centuries, simplified for your wall, affordable enough to collect.* That is its own kind of democracy.
