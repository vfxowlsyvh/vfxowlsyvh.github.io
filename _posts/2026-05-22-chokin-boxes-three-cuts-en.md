![Three Chokin boxes with decorated metal lids and ceramic bases](/assets/images/chokin-boxes-4.jpg)

These are not plates. They are boxes — small round containers with ceramic bases and metal lids, made in Japan during the 1970s or 1980s for the export market. Each lid carries a different scene and a different technique. The bases are plain porcelain: white, black, and white with a blue band. The craft lives entirely on the surface of the lid.

![Chokin box lid showing Mount Fuji landscape with dark patinated background](/assets/images/chokin-boxes-1.jpg)

The first lid uses the most traditional approach. A dark patinated metal — probably shakudo or treated copper — serves as the background. Into this dark field, the artisan has inlaid gold and silver to create a landscape: Mount Fuji in the distance, boats on water, buildings in the foreground. The technique is classic zogan, the undercut inlay method where soft precious metal is hammered into angled cavities and locked by geometry alone. Fine engraving adds detail to the architecture and waves. The dark background makes the gold read as light, the silver as water, creating depth through contrast rather than shading.

![Chokin box lid showing swans and iris flowers on light metal background](/assets/images/chokin-boxes-2.jpg)

The second lid abandons the dark ground. The base metal is left light, almost silvery, and the scene is built through engraving alone. Two swans float among iris flowers. The feathers are rendered with hair-fine lines — kebori — varying in depth and density to create texture. Color appears in the irises: soft pinks and purples that suggest irogane patination or perhaps very thin applied foils. Without a dark background, the composition relies on line quality and selective color. It reads more like a drawing and less like a relief.

![Chokin box lid showing landscape with bridge, golden tones and blue rimmed base](/assets/images/chokin-boxes-3.jpg)

The third lid is the most divergent. The background is not metal at all, or at least not visibly so. It reads as a golden ceramic or enamel surface, powdery and uniform, onto which metal elements have been placed or printed. The bridge, the trees, the houses — these may be etched, stamped, or overlaid. The texture is flatter, the lines broader, the overall effect closer to a decorative plate than to hand-chiseled metalwork. The blue-rimmed ceramic base reinforces the impression that this piece comes from a different workshop or a different production line, one less concerned with the exacting standards of Kaga inlay and more focused on volume and visual impact.

![Three opened Chokin boxes showing ceramic bases and metal lids separated](/assets/images/chokin-boxes-5.jpg)

Opened, the boxes reveal their dual nature. The lids are metal, cold and slightly magnetic. The bases are ceramic, glazed and smooth. This hybrid construction is practical: metal is difficult to form into deep cylindrical shapes, while ceramic is cheap, consistent, and easy to glaze. The combination allowed factories to produce these objects at scale while keeping the Chokin decoration on a flat, manageable surface. The artisan did not have to chase metal around a corner or worry about a base warping. They simply engraved a disc and glued it to a pot.

Collecting Chokin export ware means accepting this industrial reality. These are not one-of-a-kind museum pieces. They are factory products with hand-finished elements. But the hand-finishing is real. Someone held a chisel. Someone chose where the gold went. Someone decided that the swan's neck should curve just so. The ceramic base may be anonymous, but the lid carries the trace of a specific hand at a specific moment. That is the bargain: machine-made bones, hand-carved skin.

*Chokin boxes with metal lids and ceramic bases. Japanese export ware, 1970s–1980s. Subjects: Mount Fuji landscape, swans with irises, bridge and village. Techniques vary from traditional zogan inlay to engraved line work to surface-printed decoration.*
