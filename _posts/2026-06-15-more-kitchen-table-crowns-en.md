The maker has produced two more pieces in the same rough-gem, Han-dynasty spirit: rectangular brass trays, three stones each, no polish fetish, no certificate anxiety. If the first batch was a traffic light, this pair is a jeweler's paint box knocked over. Same honesty, louder colors.

---

## Pendant One: The Crown Jewels on a Budget

The first new pendant is green, pink-red, and deep blue — a combination that would make a Renaissance painter jealous. The green stone is the boss: a large, slightly domed rectangle of saturated green with a waxy translucence. It could be **jadeite**, **chrysoprase**, or a very clean piece of **green chalcedony**. Whatever it is, it sits there like a small lawn in a gold frame.

The middle stone is pink-red and rough-faceted, with the kind of uneven surface that tells you it was cut for shape, not for sparkle. It reads like a rough **ruby** or **pink tourmaline** — something with enough hardness to take a facet but not enough vanity to demand a mirror finish. The color is the important part: a saturated magenta that refuses to behave.

The two small blue stones on the end are almost certainly more **rough sapphire**, continuing the theme from the earlier pieces. They are dark, included, and faceted just enough to catch a glint. They do not try to compete with the green giant. They are the punctuation at the end of the sentence.

![The green-centered pendant from a different angle](/assets/images/homemade-pendant-jewel-bar-2.jpg)

Laid flat, the pendant looks like a miniature altarpiece: green field, red heart, blue feet. The brass bezel is the same simple channel as before — workmanlike, modest, slightly darkening at the edges. It does not need to be precious metal. The stones are doing all the talking.

![The green-centered pendant on its chain, showing scale](/assets/images/homemade-pendant-jewel-bar-3.jpg)

## Pendant Two: The Cool Jewel

The second piece swaps the crown-jewel palette for something colder: a large aqua-blue dome, a dark purple faceted stone, and a pink-red stone that echoes the first pendant. It is the same format wearing a different mood.

The aqua stone is the immediate relative of the **blue chalcedony** in the earlier piece — that same clean, swimming-pool blue, domed and glassy. If anything, this one is even more translucent, almost glowing at the edges where the light passes through.

The dark purple stone is almost certainly **amethyst**. It is cut in the same rough, faceted style, with surfaces that catch light unevenly. You can see the color saturation is deepest at the center and lighter toward the edges, which is typical of amethyst that has not been treated to look artificially uniform.

The pink-red stone is again likely a rough **ruby** or **pink tourmaline**. It pairs with the purple in a way that should clash but does not — the two stones seem to have an understanding.

![The aqua-centered pendant showing blue chalcedony, amethyst, and pink stone](/assets/images/homemade-pendant-aqua-purple-1.jpg)

From the side the aqua stone looks like a bead of solid water sitting on top of darker matter. The purple and pink stones underneath are almost recessed, as if the maker wanted the blue to float above them.

![The aqua-centered pendant at an angle](/assets/images/homemade-pendant-aqua-purple-2.jpg)

Turned slightly, the whole piece reads like a vertical sunset in reverse: water, twilight, last ember. It is the cooler, quieter sibling of the green-and-red piece, but it has the same stubborn refusal to be polished into submission.

![The aqua-centered pendant hanging from its brass chain](/assets/images/homemade-pendant-aqua-purple-3.jpg)

## The Same Rule Applies

These two pieces extend the argument made by the first batch. The value is not in clarity, cut grade, or carat weight. The value is in the assembly: someone looked at these rough, inexpensive stones, saw that the colors worked together, and set them without apologizing for their imperfections.

It is a small rebellion against the jewelry counter, where a stone's worth is measured by how much of it has been removed. Here the material is kept whole, included, and uneven. The brass will tarnish. The stones will scratch. The piece will look better in ten years than it does today. That is the whole philosophy in two pendants.
